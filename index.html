Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to common questions about our tutoring services

How do I schedule a tutoring session?

You can schedule a session by contacting us through our contact form, calling us directly, or using our online booking system. We offer flexible scheduling to accommodate your needs.

What qualifications do your tutors have?

All our tutors hold advanced degrees in mathematics or related fields from top universities. They have extensive teaching experience and undergo regular training to ensure the highest quality instruction.

How do you incorporate Mongolian culture into lessons?

We weave Mongolian culture into math problems and examples—like using ger geometry for circle problems, shagai (ankle bones) for probability, or herding scenarios for algebra. This makes learning more meaningful and helps students stay connected to their heritage.

What subjects do you cover?

We cover all mathematics subjects from elementary school through college level, including algebra, geometry, calculus, statistics, trigonometry, and more specialized topics.

Do you offer in-person tutoring?

We do not offer in-person tutoring for now. All our sessions are conducted online, which allows us to serve Mongolian students worldwide with flexible scheduling across different time zones.

How do you track student progress?

We use comprehensive progress tracking that includes regular assessments, homework completion rates, test scores, and detailed reports shared with both students and parents.

Math, Rooted in Mongolian Life

We enrich core skills with examples drawn from Mongolian culture so concepts feel relevant and memorable.

Geometry: The Ger

A traditional ger has radius 5 m, and the door is 1 m wide. Approximate the busluur (crossbeam) length across the doorway. Students apply circle geometry and chords in context.

Probability: Shagai

If four ankle bones are tossed, what is the probability all four show distinct animals? Learners analyze outcomes and independence using a familiar game.

Algebra: Herd Planning

A herder increases goats by 12% and sheep by 8% year‑over‑year. Build a model to project totals and feed usage—connecting linear and exponential reasoning.
