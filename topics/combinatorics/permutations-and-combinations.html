Permutations & Combinations

The fundamental principles of counting and arranging objects

Home » Learning Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Combinatorics » Permutations & Combinations

The Fundamental Counting Principle

The Fundamental Counting Principle (also known as the multiplication principle) forms the basis of combinatorial counting. It states that if one event can occur in m ways, and a second event can occur in n ways, then the number of ways both events can occur is m × n.

If task 1 can be done in n₁ ways, task 2 in n₂ ways, ..., and task k in nₖ ways, then the sequence of tasks can be performed in n₁ × n₂ × ... × nₖ different ways.

Example: Applying the Fundamental Counting Principle

A restaurant offers 4 appetizers, 6 main courses, and 3 desserts. How many different three-course meals can be created?

Using the Fundamental Counting Principle:

Number of possible meals = Number of appetizers × Number of main courses × Number of desserts

Number of possible meals = 4 × 6 × 3 = 72

Therefore, 72 different three-course meals can be created.

Permutations

A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects is n! (n factorial).

n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 3 × 2 × 1

By convention, 0! = 1

Permutations of n Objects Taken r at a Time

The number of ways to arrange r objects selected from n distinct objects, where order matters, is denoted by P(n,r) or nPr.

P(n,r) = nPr = n!/(n-r)!

Example: Calculating Permutations

In how many ways can we arrange 3 books on a shelf if we have 7 different books?

This is a permutation problem where we need to find P(7,3):

P(7,3) = 7!/(7-3)! = 7!/4!

P(7,3) = (7 × 6 × 5 × 4!)/(4!) = 7 × 6 × 5 = 210

Therefore, there are 210 different ways to arrange 3 books from a collection of 7 books.

Permutations with Repetition

If we have n objects, where there are n₁ objects of type 1, n₂ objects of type 2, ..., nₖ objects of type k, then the number of distinct permutations is:

Number of permutations = n!/(n₁! × n₂! × ... × nₖ!)

Example: Permutations with Repetition

How many different arrangements can be made from the letters of the word "MONGOLIA"?

The word "MONGOLIA" has 8 letters, with:

- 1 'M'
- 1 'I'
- 2 'O's
- 1 'N'
- 1 'G'
- 1 'L'
- 1 'A'

Using the formula for permutations with repetition:

Number of permutations = 8!/(1! × 1! × 2! × 1! × 1! × 1! × 1!)

Number of permutations = 8!/(1 × 1 × 2 × 1 × 1 × 1 × 1)

Number of permutations = 40,320/2 = 20,160

Therefore, there are 20,160 different arrangements of the letters in "MONGOLIA".

Combinations

A combination is a selection of objects where the order does not matter. The number of ways to select r objects from n distinct objects is denoted by C(n,r) or nCr, also written as (n r) or "n choose r".

C(n,r) = nCr = (n r) = n!/[r!(n-r)!]

Properties of Combinations

  • C(n,0) = C(n,n) = 1
  • C(n,r) = C(n,n-r)
  • C(n,r) + C(n,r-1) = C(n+1,r)
Example: Calculating Combinations

A traditional Mongolian wrestling tournament needs to select 4 wrestlers from a group of 9 competitors to represent their province. How many different teams are possible?

This is a combination problem since the order of the team members doesn't matter. We need to find C(9,4):

C(9,4) = 9!/[4!(9-4)!] = 9!/(4! × 5!)

C(9,4) = (9 × 8 × 7 × 6 × 5!)/(4 × 3 × 2 × 1 × 5!)

C(9,4) = (9 × 8 × 7 × 6)/(4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 3,024/24 = 126

Therefore, 126 different wrestling teams can be formed.

Combinations with Repetition

The number of ways to select r objects from n distinct objects, where repetition is allowed, is given by:

C(n+r-1,r) = (n+r-1)!/(r! × (n-1)!)

Example: Combinations with Repetition

A Mongolian dairy farmer has 5 different types of dairy products (airag, tarag, aaruul, byaslag, and eezgii). How many ways can they select 8 items for a traditional feast if they can choose multiple of the same type?

This is a combination with repetition allowed. We need to find C(5+8-1,8) = C(12,8):

C(12,8) = 12!/[8!(12-8)!] = 12!/(8! × 4!)

C(12,8) = (12 × 11 × 10 × 9 × 8!)/(8! × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1)

C(12,8) = (12 × 11 × 10 × 9)/(4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 11,880/24 = 495

Therefore, there are 495 different ways to select 8 dairy products from 5 types.

Distinguishing Permutations from Combinations

Understanding when to use permutations versus combinations is crucial in solving counting problems:

  • Use permutations when:
    • Order matters (e.g., arranging books on a shelf, ranking competitors)
    • The problem involves sequences, arrangements, or ordered selections
    • Words like "arrange," "order," "sequence," or "ranking" appear in the problem
  • Use combinations when:
    • Order doesn't matter (e.g., selecting a committee, choosing items)
    • The problem involves groups, teams, or unordered selections
    • Words like "select," "choose," "committee," or "group" appear in the problem
Example: Distinguishing Between Permutations and Combinations

Consider these two scenarios:

1. How many ways can 3 archers from a group of 25 be selected to receive gold, silver, and bronze medals in a traditional Mongolian archery competition?

2. How many ways can 3 archers from a group of 25 be selected to form a team?

For scenario 1, the order matters because each archer receives a specific medal. This is a permutation: P(25,3) = 25!/(25-3)! = 25!/22! = 25 × 24 × 23 = 13,800.

For scenario 2, the order doesn't matter because the team members are all equal. This is a combination: C(25,3) = 25!/[3!(25-3)!] = 25!/(3! × 22!) = (25 × 24 × 23)/(3 × 2 × 1) = 13,800/6 = 2,300.

Applications in Problem Solving

1. Combinatorial Probability

Permutations and combinations are essential in calculating probabilities for many random experiments.

Example: Probability Using Combinations

In a card game using 52 cards, what is the probability of being dealt a hand of 5 cards that contains exactly 2 aces?

Total number of 5-card hands = C(52,5)

Number of hands with exactly 2 aces = C(4,2) × C(48,3)

C(4,2) = 6 (ways to choose 2 aces from 4 aces)

C(48,3) = 17,296 (ways to choose 3 non-aces from 48 non-aces)

Number of hands with exactly 2 aces = 6 × 17,296 = 103,776

C(52,5) = 2,598,960

Probability = 103,776/2,598,960 ≈ 0.0399 or about 4%

2. Counting Problems

Many real-world problems involve counting the number of possible arrangements or selections.

Example: License Plate Problem

A license plate consists of 3 letters followed by 4 digits. How many different license plates are possible if:

a) Repetition is allowed for both letters and digits

b) Repetition is not allowed for letters but allowed for digits

a) With repetition allowed:

Number of ways to choose 3 letters with repetition allowed = 26³ = 17,576

Number of ways to choose 4 digits with repetition allowed = 10⁴ = 10,000

Total number of possible license plates = 17,576 × 10,000 = 175,760,000

b) With no repetition for letters but repetition allowed for digits:

Number of ways to choose 3 letters with no repetition = P(26,3) = 26 × 25 × 24 = 15,600

Number of ways to choose 4 digits with repetition allowed = 10⁴ = 10,000

Total number of possible license plates = 15,600 × 10,000 = 156,000,000
