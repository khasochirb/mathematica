A powerful tool for proving the existence of patterns

Basic Pigeonhole Principle

The Pigeonhole Principle, also known as the Dirichlet box principle, is a simple yet powerful mathematical concept used to prove the existence of certain patterns or properties without directly constructing them.

Statement of the Principle

The basic form of the pigeonhole principle states:

If n pigeons are placed into m pigeonholes, and n > m, then at least one pigeonhole must contain more than one pigeon.

More precisely, at least one pigeonhole must contain at least ⌈n/m⌉ pigeons, where ⌈x⌉ represents the ceiling function (the smallest integer greater than or equal to x).

Example: Basic Pigeonhole Principle

In a traditional Mongolian wrestling tournament with 13 wrestlers, at least 2 wrestlers must have the same number of wins (assuming each wrestler has at least one win).

Proof:

If each wrestler has at least one win, the number of wins for each wrestler must be between 1 and 12 (since there are 13 wrestlers, a wrestler can't have more wins than there are other wrestlers).

We have 13 wrestlers (pigeons) and 12 possible numbers of wins (pigeonholes: 1 through 12).

By the pigeonhole principle, at least 2 wrestlers must have the same number of wins.

Example: Socks in a Drawer

A drawer contains 10 blue socks and 10 black socks that have been mixed up. How many socks must you take out (in the dark) to guarantee getting a matching pair?

There are 2 colors (pigeonholes), and we need to ensure we have at least 2 socks of the same color. By the pigeonhole principle, we need to take at least 2 + 1 = 3 socks to guarantee a matching pair.

With 3 socks, even in the worst case (1 blue sock and 1 black sock initially), the third sock must match one of the first two.

Generalized Pigeonhole Principle

The generalized pigeonhole principle extends the basic principle to situations where we want to guarantee a certain number of items in a single category.

If n pigeons are placed into m pigeonholes, then at least one pigeonhole must contain at least ⌈n/m⌉ pigeons.

Example: Generalized Pigeonhole Principle

In an archery competition, if 25 archers shoot at targets with 4 different scoring zones, at least 7 archers must hit the same scoring zone.

Proof:

We have 25 archers (pigeons) and 4 scoring zones (pigeonholes).

By the generalized pigeonhole principle, at least ⌈25/4⌉ = 7 archers must hit the same scoring zone.

Example: Students and Grades

In a class of 50 students, grades are given as A, B, C, D, or F. Prove that at least 10 students must receive the same grade.

Here, we have 50 students (pigeons) and 5 possible grades (pigeonholes).

By the generalized pigeonhole principle, at least one grade must be given to at least ⌈50/5⌉ = 10 students.

Example: Selecting Numbers

From the set {1, 2, 3, ..., 20}, what is the minimum number of integers you need to select to guarantee that at least 3 of them have the same remainder when divided by 6?

When dividing by 6, there are 6 possible remainders: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 (pigeonholes).

To guarantee that at least 3 numbers have the same remainder, we need to select enough numbers so that at least one remainder appears 3 times.

By the generalized principle, we need at least 6(3-1) + 1 = 13 numbers.

Why? In the worst case, we could select 2 numbers from each of the 6 remainder classes, giving us 12 numbers with no three having the same remainder. The 13th number must fall into one of the 6 remainder classes, giving us 3 numbers with the same remainder.

Strong Form of the Pigeonhole Principle

The strong form of the pigeonhole principle deals with situations where we have multiple constraints or want to ensure multiple items share properties.

If n items are placed into m categories, and n > mk, then at least one category must contain at least k+1 items.

Example: Strong Form

In a horse racing competition, if 10 horses race in 3 heats, there must be a heat where at least 4 horses participate.

Proof:

If we distribute 10 horses among 3 heats, and no heat has 4 or more horses, then each heat has at most 3 horses.

This would mean there are at most 3 × 3 = 9 horses total, which contradicts the fact that we have 10 horses.

Therefore, at least one heat must have 4 or more horses.

Example: Subset Sum Problem

Given any set of n integers, prove that there exists a non-empty subset whose sum is divisible by n.

Consider the partial sums: s₁ = a₁, s₂ = a₁ + a₂, ..., sₙ = a₁ + a₂ + ... + aₙ.

By the pigeonhole principle, among these n sums, at least two must have the same remainder when divided by n (since there are only n possible remainders: 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1).

If sᵢ and sⱼ have the same remainder (with i < j), then sⱼ - sᵢ = aᵢ₊₁ + ... + aⱼ is divisible by n.

Therefore, the subset {aᵢ₊₁, ..., aⱼ} has a sum divisible by n.

Example: Points in a Plane

Prove that among any 5 points in a unit square, there must be 2 points that are at most √2/2 ≈ 0.707 units apart.

Divide the unit square into 4 equal subsquares, each with side length 1/2.

By the pigeonhole principle, when 5 points are placed into 4 subsquares, at least one subsquare must contain at least 2 points.

The maximum distance between any two points in a square of side length 1/2 is the diagonal, which is (1/2)·√2 = √2/2.

Therefore, the two points in the same subsquare are at most √2/2 units apart.

Applications in Mathematics

Number Theory

The pigeonhole principle is often used in number theory to prove the existence of certain numbers with specific properties.

Example: Decimal Representation

Prove that a fraction a/b, where a and b are coprime positive integers, has a repeating decimal expansion if and only if b has a prime factorization containing only 2s and 5s.

When computing a/b as a decimal, we continually multiply the remainder by 10 and take the integer part as the next digit.

Since the remainder is always between 0 and b-1, there are only b possible remainders.

By the pigeonhole principle, if we calculate more than b digits, we must encounter the same remainder twice, which causes the decimal expansion to repeat from that point.

The only fractions that don't produce a repeating decimal are those where b is a divisor of some power of 10, which means b can only have prime factors of 2 and 5.

Example: Number Theory Application

In a musical performance, if 8 musicians play notes from a scale of 7 notes, at least 2 musicians must play the same note at some point.

Proof:

Consider the sequence of notes played by each musician as a sequence of integers from 1 to 7.

If we look at the first 8 notes played (one from each musician), we have 8 integers (pigeons) and 7 possible values (pigeonholes).

By the pigeonhole principle, at least 2 musicians must play the same note.

Ramsey Theory

The pigeonhole principle is fundamental to Ramsey theory, which studies the conditions under which order must appear in disordered structures.

Example: Friendship Theorem

In a party of 6 people, prove that there must be either 3 mutual strangers or 3 mutual acquaintances.

Represent people as vertices and relationships as edges in a graph, where an edge can be either "acquaintance" (blue) or "stranger" (red).

Consider any person at the party. There are 5 other people, each with a relationship to our selected person.

By the pigeonhole principle, at least 3 of these relationships must be of the same type (either all "acquaintance" or all "stranger").

Let's assume 3 people are acquaintances of our selected person. If any 2 of these 3 people are acquaintances of each other, then together with our selected person, they form a group of 3 mutual acquaintances.

If none of these 3 people are acquaintances of each other, then they form a group of 3 mutual strangers.

A similar argument works if 3 people are strangers to our selected person.

Therefore, there must exist either 3 mutual acquaintances or 3 mutual strangers.

Applications in Computer Science

Algorithm Analysis

The pigeonhole principle is used in the analysis of algorithms, particularly for proving lower bounds on algorithm complexity.

Example: Algorithm Analysis

In a card game, if a player draws 5 cards from a deck of 52 cards, they must have at least 2 cards of the same suit.

Proof:

There are 4 suits in a deck (pigeonholes) and 5 cards drawn (pigeons).

By the pigeonhole principle, at least ⌈5/4⌉ = 2 cards must be of the same suit.

This is a fundamental result used in analyzing card game algorithms and probabilities.

Hashing

The pigeonhole principle explains why collisions are inevitable in hash functions when the input domain is larger than the output range.

Example: Hash Collisions

A hash function maps arbitrary-sized inputs to fixed-size outputs. If the input space exceeds the output space, show that collisions must exist.

Let's say we have a hash function h that maps inputs from a set S to outputs in a set T, where |S| > |T|.

By the pigeonhole principle, if we hash more than |T| distinct inputs, at least two inputs must hash to the same output value.

This is why all practical hash functions have collisions, and why hash table implementations must handle collisions.

The Pigeonhole Principle in Everyday Life

The pigeonhole principle appears in many common situations, often where we need to prove something must happen without specifying exactly when or how.

Example: Identical Hairs

The average human head has about 100,000 hairs, while hair colors are typically classified into a few dozen shades. By the pigeonhole principle, many hairs on a person's head must be of identical color.

Example: Calendar Events

If a person schedules 366 different events over the course of a year, prove that at least 2 events must occur on the same day.

Even in a leap year, there are at most 366 days. With 366 events and 366 days, each day could contain exactly one event.

However, with 367 events, by the pigeonhole principle, at least one day must contain at least 2 events.

Example: Matching Birthdays

In a room of 23 people, what is the probability that at least 2 people share the same birthday (ignoring leap years)?

This is not directly a pigeonhole principle problem since we have 23 people and 365 days, but it illustrates how the principle applies to probability.

The probability that all 23 people have different birthdays is:
P(all different) = (365/365) × (364/365) × ... × (343/365) ≈ 0.493

Therefore, the probability that at least 2 people share a birthday is:
P(at least one match) = 1 - P(all different) ≈ 1 - 0.493 = 0.507 or about 50.7%

This result is surprisingly high, which shows the power of the pigeonhole principle in real-world scenarios.
