De Moivre's Theorem

A powerful tool for calculating powers and roots of complex numbers

Home » Learning Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Complex Numbers » De Moivre's Theorem

Introduction to De Moivre's Theorem

De Moivre's Theorem, named after Abraham de Moivre (1667-1754), provides a formula for computing integer powers of complex numbers written in polar form. This theorem is one of the most important results in complex number theory and has applications in various fields including trigonometry, calculus, and physics.

Statement of the Theorem

For any complex number z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) and any integer n, De Moivre's Theorem states:

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^n = r^n(cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ))

Using the more compact "cis" notation, this becomes:

[r cis θ]^n = r^n cis(nθ)

In exponential form, the theorem is elegantly expressed as:

[r·e^(iθ)]^n = r^n·e^(inθ)

Example: Using De Moivre's Theorem

Find (1 + i)⁶.

First, express 1 + i in polar form:

|1 + i| = √(1² + 1²) = √2

arg(1 + i) = tan⁻¹(1/1) = π/4

So 1 + i = √2 cis(π/4).

Using De Moivre's Theorem:

(1 + i)⁶ = [√2 cis(π/4)]⁶ = (√2)⁶ cis(6π/4) = 8 cis(6π/4) = 8 cis(3π/2)

Converting back to rectangular form:

8 cis(3π/2) = 8 cos(3π/2) + 8i sin(3π/2) = 8(0) + 8i(-1) = -8i

Proof of De Moivre's Theorem

We can prove De Moivre's Theorem by mathematical induction.

Base Case: n = 1

For n = 1, we have:

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]¹ = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r cos θ + i r sin θ = r(cos(1·θ) + i sin(1·θ))

So the theorem holds for n = 1.

Inductive Step

Assume the theorem holds for some positive integer k:

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^k = r^k(cos(kθ) + i sin(kθ))

Now we need to show it holds for n = k + 1:

[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^(k+1) = [r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^k · [r(cos θ + i sin θ)]

= r^k(cos(kθ) + i sin(kθ)) · r(cos θ + i sin θ)

= r^(k+1)[(cos(kθ) + i sin(kθ))(cos θ + i sin θ)]

Using the multiplication rule for complex numbers in polar form:

= r^(k+1)[cos(kθ)cos θ - sin(kθ)sin θ + i(sin(kθ)cos θ + cos(kθ)sin θ)]

Using the cosine and sine addition formulas:

= r^(k+1)[cos(kθ + θ) + i sin(kθ + θ)]

= r^(k+1)[cos((k+1)θ) + i sin((k+1)θ)]

This completes the inductive step. By mathematical induction, De Moivre's Theorem is valid for all positive integers n.

For negative integers, we can use the fact that z^(-n) = 1/(z^n) and apply the theorem to the denominator.

Applications in Trigonometry

De Moivre's Theorem provides an elegant way to derive many important trigonometric identities.

Deriving Trigonometric Formulas

By expanding [cos θ + i sin θ]^n using the binomial theorem and comparing the real and imaginary parts with cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ), we can derive formulas for cos(nθ) and sin(nθ) in terms of powers of cos θ and sin θ.

[cos θ + i sin θ]^n = cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)

= Σ(k=0 to n) (n choose k) (cos θ)^(n-k) (i sin θ)^k

Equating the real and imaginary parts gives:

cos(nθ) = Σ(k=0 to [n/2]) (-1)^k (n choose 2k) (cos θ)^(n-2k) (sin θ)^(2k)

sin(nθ) = Σ(k=0 to [(n-1)/2]) (-1)^k (n choose 2k+1) (cos θ)^(n-2k-1) (sin θ)^(2k+1)

These formulas allow us to express cos(nθ) and sin(nθ) as polynomials in cos θ and sin θ.

Example: Deriving Trigonometric Identities

Let's derive the formula for cos(3θ) using De Moivre's Theorem.

We have [cos θ + i sin θ]^3 = cos(3θ) + i sin(3θ).

Expanding the left side using the binomial theorem:

[cos θ + i sin θ]^3 = (cos θ)^3 + 3(cos θ)^2(i sin θ) + 3(cos θ)(i sin θ)^2 + (i sin θ)^3

= (cos θ)^3 + 3(cos θ)^2(i sin θ) + 3(cos θ)(-sin θ)^2 + (i sin θ)^3

= (cos θ)^3 - 3(cos θ)(sin θ)^2 + i[3(cos θ)^2(sin θ) - (sin θ)^3]

Equating the real parts:

cos(3θ) = (cos θ)^3 - 3(cos θ)(sin θ)^2

Using the identity sin²θ = 1 - cos²θ:

cos(3θ) = (cos θ)^3 - 3(cos θ)(1 - cos²θ)

= (cos θ)^3 - 3(cos θ) + 3(cos θ)^3

= 4(cos θ)^3 - 3(cos θ)

Finding Roots of Complex Numbers

One of the most important applications of De Moivre's Theorem is finding complex nth roots. If z = r cis θ is a complex number, then its nth roots are given by:

w_k = r^(1/n) cis((θ + 2kπ)/n)

where k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1.

This formula gives us exactly n distinct nth roots for any non-zero complex number.

Geometric Interpretation

Geometrically, the n distinct nth roots of a complex number lie evenly spaced on a circle centered at the origin. The radius of this circle is r^(1/n), and the arguments of the roots are spaced 2π/n radians apart.

Example: Finding Cube Roots

Find all the cube roots of z = 8(cos(π/3) + i sin(π/3)).

We have z = 8 cis(π/3) and need to find all w such that w³ = z.

Using the formula for nth roots:

w_k = 8^(1/3) cis((π/3 + 2kπ)/3) = 2 cis((π/3 + 2kπ)/3)

For k = 0: w₀ = 2 cis(π/9) = 2(cos(π/9) + i sin(π/9)) ≈ 1.97 + 0.35i

For k = 1: w₁ = 2 cis(π/9 + 2π/3) = 2 cis(7π/9) = 2(cos(7π/9) + i sin(7π/9)) ≈ 0.43 + 1.95i

For k = 2: w₂ = 2 cis(π/9 + 4π/3) = 2 cis(13π/9) = 2(cos(13π/9) + i sin(13π/9)) ≈ -2.4 + 0.73i

Verification: (w₀)³ = (w₁)³ = (w₂)³ = 8 cis(π/3) = z

nth Roots of Unity

A special case of finding complex roots is determining the nth roots of unity, which are the solutions to the equation z^n = 1.

The nth roots of unity are given by:

ω_k = cis(2πk/n)

where k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1.

These roots have special properties:

  • They form a cyclic group under multiplication
  • The principal nth root of unity, ω = cis(2π/n), is a generator of this group
  • The sum of all nth roots of unity is 0 for n > 1
  • They have applications in various fields, including number theory, Fourier analysis, and discrete signal processing
Example: 4th Roots of Unity

Find all the 4th roots of unity.

Using the formula for nth roots of unity:

ω_k = cis(2πk/4) = cis(πk/2)

For k = 0: ω₀ = cis(0) = 1

For k = 1: ω₁ = cis(π/2) = i

For k = 2: ω₂ = cis(π) = -1

For k = 3: ω₃ = cis(3π/2) = -i

These four complex numbers are the 4th roots of unity. Note that they form the vertices of a square centered at the origin in the complex plane.

Practical Applications

Signal Processing

De Moivre's Theorem and complex roots of unity play a crucial role in signal processing, particularly in the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm. The FFT efficiently computes the Discrete Fourier Transform of a sequence, which decomposes a signal into its frequency components.

Control Theory

In control theory, complex numbers are used to analyze the stability of systems. De Moivre's Theorem helps in understanding the behavior of systems by finding the roots of characteristic polynomials.

Electrical Engineering

Complex numbers represent impedance, and De Moivre's Theorem is used to analyze AC circuits, especially when calculating power and converting between series and parallel combinations of components.

Computer Graphics

Rotations in 2D space can be represented using complex numbers. De Moivre's Theorem allows for efficient computation of multiple rotations, which is useful in computer graphics and animation.

Example: Electrical Engineering Application

In an AC circuit with impedance Z = 3 + 4i ohms, calculate the impedance after adding five identical components in series.

For components in series, impedances add:

Z_total = 5Z = 5(3 + 4i) = 15 + 20i ohms

If we wanted to find the equivalent of raising the impedance to a power (as in some network analyses):

First, express Z in polar form: Z = 3 + 4i = 5 cis(tan⁻¹(4/3)) ≈ 5 cis(0.9273)

Using De Moivre's Theorem: Z^5 = 5^5 cis(5 × 0.9273) ≈ 3125 cis(4.6365)

Converting back to rectangular form: Z^5 ≈ 3125(cos(4.6365) + i sin(4.6365)) ≈ -2956 + 1067i ohms
