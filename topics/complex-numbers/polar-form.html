Polar Form & Argand Plane

Exploring complex numbers through geometric representation

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Complex Numbers » Polar Form & Argand Plane

The Argand Plane

The Argand plane (also called the complex plane) is a geometric representation of complex numbers as points on a two-dimensional coordinate system, where:

  • The horizontal axis represents the real part of the complex number
  • The vertical axis represents the imaginary part of the complex number

Each complex number z = a + bi is represented as the point (a, b) on this plane. This representation allows us to visualize complex numbers and understand their geometric properties.

Geometric Interpretation

In the Argand plane:

  • The distance from the origin to the point (a, b) is the modulus or absolute value |z| = √(a² + b²)
  • The angle formed by the positive real axis and the line segment from the origin to the point (a, b) is the argument of z, denoted by arg(z)
  • Addition of complex numbers corresponds to vector addition in the plane
  • Multiplication by a complex number corresponds to a rotation and scaling in the plane
Example: Geometric Representation

Consider the complex number z = 3 + 4i.

On the Argand plane, this is represented as the point (3, 4).

The modulus is |z| = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5.

The argument is arg(z) = tan⁻¹(4/3) ≈ 53.13°.

Polar Form of Complex Numbers

Every complex number can be written in polar form, which emphasizes its geometric properties in the Argand plane.

Converting from Rectangular to Polar Form

For a complex number z = a + bi in rectangular form, the polar form is:

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r cis θ

Where:

  • r = |z| = √(a² + b²) is the modulus or absolute value
  • θ = arg(z) is the argument or angle, typically given in radians
  • "cis" is shorthand for "cos + i sin"

The argument θ can be calculated as:

θ = arg(z) = tan⁻¹(b/a)

However, care must be taken with the above formula to account for the quadrant in which the point (a, b) lies:

  • If a > 0, b > 0 (first quadrant): θ = tan⁻¹(b/a)
  • If a < 0 (second or third quadrant): θ = tan⁻¹(b/a) + π
  • If a > 0, b < 0 (fourth quadrant): θ = tan⁻¹(b/a) + 2π
  • If a = 0, b > 0: θ = π/2
  • If a = 0, b < 0: θ = 3π/2
  • If a = 0, b = 0: θ is undefined (z = 0)

Many programming languages provide an atan2(y, x) function that correctly handles all these cases.

Example: Converting to Polar Form

Express z = -3 + 4i in polar form.

First, calculate the modulus: r = |z| = √((-3)² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5.

Next, calculate the argument: θ = tan⁻¹(4/(-3)).

Since a < 0 and b > 0 (second quadrant), we add π to the result:

θ = tan⁻¹(-4/3) + π ≈ -53.13° + 180° ≈ 126.87° ≈ 2.214 radians.

Therefore, in polar form: z = 5(cos(2.214) + i sin(2.214)) = 5 cis(2.214).

Converting from Polar to Rectangular Form

If we have a complex number z = r cis θ in polar form, we can convert it to rectangular form using:

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r cos θ + i r sin θ

Example: Converting to Rectangular Form

Express z = 6 cis(π/3) in rectangular form.

We have r = 6 and θ = π/3 (60°).

The real part is a = r cos θ = 6 cos(π/3) = 6 × (1/2) = 3.

The imaginary part is b = r sin θ = 6 sin(π/3) = 6 × (√3/2) = 3√3.

Therefore, in rectangular form: z = 3 + 3√3i.

Operations in Polar Form

The polar form of complex numbers is particularly useful for multiplication, division, powers, and roots.

Multiplication in Polar Form

To multiply two complex numbers in polar form:

z₁ = r₁ cis θ₁

z₂ = r₂ cis θ₂

z₁ × z₂ = r₁r₂ cis(θ₁ + θ₂)

This shows that when multiplying complex numbers:

  • The moduli are multiplied: r = r₁ × r₂
  • The arguments are added: θ = θ₁ + θ₂
Example: Multiplication in Polar Form

Multiply z₁ = 2 cis(π/4) and z₂ = 3 cis(π/6).

Using the formula for multiplication in polar form:

z₁ × z₂ = 2 × 3 cis(π/4 + π/6) = 6 cis(5π/12)

Converting back to rectangular form:

6 cis(5π/12) = 6 cos(5π/12) + 6i sin(5π/12) ≈ 3.83 + 4.63i

Division in Polar Form

To divide two complex numbers in polar form:

z₁ = r₁ cis θ₁

z₂ = r₂ cis θ₂

z₁ ÷ z₂ = (r₁/r₂) cis(θ₁ - θ₂)

This shows that when dividing complex numbers:

  • The moduli are divided: r = r₁ ÷ r₂
  • The arguments are subtracted: θ = θ₁ - θ₂
Example: Division in Polar Form

Divide z₁ = 8 cis(2π/3) by z₂ = 2 cis(π/6).

Using the formula for division in polar form:

z₁ ÷ z₂ = (8/2) cis(2π/3 - π/6) = 4 cis(π/2) = 4 cis(90°)

Converting back to rectangular form:

4 cis(90°) = 4 cos(90°) + 4i sin(90°) = 0 + 4i = 4i

Exponential Form

Another representation of complex numbers is the exponential form, based on Euler's formula:

e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ

Using this formula, a complex number in polar form can be written as:

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r·e^(iθ)

This exponential form is particularly useful for powers and roots of complex numbers.

Euler's Identity

One of the most famous results in mathematics is Euler's identity, which is obtained by setting θ = π in Euler's formula:

e^(iπ) + 1 = 0

This elegant equation connects five fundamental mathematical constants: e, i, π, 1, and 0, each appearing exactly once.

Example: Using Exponential Form

Express z = 5 cis(π/3) in exponential form.

Using Euler's formula:

z = 5 cis(π/3) = 5(cos(π/3) + i sin(π/3)) = 5·e^(iπ/3)

Complex Vectors and Transformations

The Argand plane allows us to interpret complex numbers as vectors and complex operations as geometric transformations.

Complex Numbers as Vectors

A complex number z = a + bi can be interpreted as the vector (a, b) in the Cartesian plane:

  • Addition of complex numbers corresponds to vector addition (head-to-tail rule)
  • Multiplication by a real number corresponds to scaling the vector

Geometric Interpretation of Multiplication

When a complex number z is multiplied by another complex number w:

  • The modulus of z is scaled by the modulus of w: |zw| = |z|·|w|
  • The argument of z is rotated by the argument of w: arg(zw) = arg(z) + arg(w)

Special cases:

  • Multiplication by i rotates a complex number 90° counterclockwise
  • Multiplication by -1 rotates a complex number 180°
  • Multiplication by -i rotates a complex number 270° counterclockwise (or 90° clockwise)
Example: Geometric Interpretation

Consider z = 3 + 4i with |z| = 5 and arg(z) ≈ 53.13°.

When multiplied by i:

i·z = i(3 + 4i) = 3i + 4i² = 3i - 4 = -4 + 3i

The new complex number has the same modulus |i·z| = 5, but its argument has increased by 90°: arg(i·z) ≈ 143.13°.

This confirms that multiplication by i corresponds to a 90° counterclockwise rotation.

Loci in the Complex Plane

Many geometric loci can be described elegantly using complex numbers:

  • |z| = r represents a circle of radius r centered at the origin
  • |z - z₀| = r represents a circle of radius r centered at z₀
  • |z - z₁| = |z - z₂| represents the perpendicular bisector of the line segment joining z₁ and z₂
  • arg(z - z₀) = θ represents a ray from z₀ at angle θ
Example: Loci in the Complex Plane

Describe the locus of points z such that |z - (1 + i)| = 2.

This represents a circle of radius 2 centered at the point 1 + i in the complex plane.

In terms of the real and imaginary parts z = x + yi, the equation becomes:

√((x - 1)² + (y - 1)²) = 2

or equivalently: (x - 1)² + (y - 1)² = 4

This is the equation of a circle with center (1, 1) and radius 2.
