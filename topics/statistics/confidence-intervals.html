Confidence Intervals

Estimating population parameters with a range of plausible values

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Statistics » Confidence Intervals

Introduction to Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of values that is likely to contain an unknown population parameter with a certain level of confidence. Unlike point estimates that give a single value for a parameter, confidence intervals provide a range of plausible values, reflecting the uncertainty in the estimation process.

Key components of a confidence interval include:

  • Point Estimate: The single best estimate of the parameter (usually at the center of the interval)
  • Margin of Error: The amount added and subtracted from the point estimate to create the interval
  • Confidence Level: The probability (typically expressed as a percentage) that the construction method produces an interval containing the true parameter value

Common confidence levels include 90%, 95%, and 99%, with 95% being the most widely used.

Example 1: Interpreting a Confidence Interval

A researcher reports that the 95% confidence interval for the mean height of adult women in a population is (163.5 cm, 166.7 cm).

Correct Interpretation: "If we were to take many random samples of the same size from this population and construct a 95% confidence interval for each sample, about 95% of these intervals would contain the true mean height of adult women in the population."

Incorrect Interpretation: "There is a 95% probability that the true mean height of adult women in the population is between 163.5 cm and 166.7 cm." (This is incorrect because the true parameter is fixed, not random.)

Constructing Confidence Intervals

The general formula for a confidence interval is:

Point Estimate ± (Critical Value × Standard Error)

The components vary depending on the parameter being estimated and the distribution of the statistic.

Confidence Interval for a Population Mean

When the population standard deviation (σ) is known (rare in practice):

x̄ ± zα/2 × (σ/√n)

Where:

  • x̄ = sample mean
  • zα/2 = critical value from the standard normal distribution
  • σ = population standard deviation
  • n = sample size

When σ is unknown (common in practice):

x̄ ± tα/2, n-1 × (s/√n)

Where:

  • s = sample standard deviation
  • tα/2, n-1 = critical value from the t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom
Example 2: Confidence Interval for a Population Mean

A random sample of 40 batteries has a mean lifetime of 215 hours with a standard deviation of 20 hours. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the mean lifetime of all batteries of this type.

Since σ is unknown, we use the t-distribution with 40-1 = 39 degrees of freedom.

For 95% confidence, t0.025, 39 = 2.023

CI = 215 ± 2.023 × (20/√40) = 215 ± 2.023 × 3.16 = 215 ± 6.39 = (208.61, 221.39)

We are 95% confident that the true mean lifetime of all batteries of this type is between 208.61 and 221.39 hours.

Other Types of Confidence Intervals

Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

p̂ ± zα/2 × √(p̂(1-p̂)/n)

Where:

  • p̂ = sample proportion (number of successes divided by sample size)
  • n = sample size

This formula is valid when np̂ ≥ 10 and n(1-p̂) ≥ 10.

Confidence Interval for the Difference Between Two Means

Independent samples with unknown but equal population variances:

(x̄₁ - x̄₂) ± tα/2, n₁+n₂-2 × sp × √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)

Where sp is the pooled standard deviation:

sp = √[((n₁-1)s₁² + (n₂-1)s₂²)/(n₁+n₂-2)]

Confidence Interval for the Difference Between Two Proportions

(p̂₁ - p̂₂) ± zα/2 × √[p̂₁(1-p̂₁)/n₁ + p̂₂(1-p̂₂)/n₂]
Example 3: Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

In a random sample of 500 voters, 280 support a particular candidate. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true proportion of voters who support this candidate.

Sample proportion p̂ = 280/500 = 0.56

For 95% confidence, z0.025 = 1.96

CI = 0.56 ± 1.96 × √[(0.56 × 0.44)/500] = 0.56 ± 1.96 × 0.0222 = 0.56 ± 0.0435 = (0.5165, 0.6035)

We are 95% confident that the true proportion of voters who support the candidate is between 51.65% and 60.35%.

Factors Affecting Confidence Interval Width

Several factors influence the width of a confidence interval:

1. Confidence Level

Higher confidence levels lead to wider intervals. For example, a 99% confidence interval is wider than a 95% confidence interval (all else being equal).

2. Sample Size

Larger sample sizes produce narrower confidence intervals because the standard error decreases as the sample size increases.

3. Population Variability

Greater population variability (larger standard deviation) results in wider confidence intervals.

4. Sample Design

Simple random sampling typically leads to wider confidence intervals than stratified or cluster sampling (for the same sample size).

Example 4: Effect of Confidence Level and Sample Size

Consider the battery example (mean = 215 hours, s = 20 hours):

Effect of Confidence Level (n = 40):

  • 90% CI: 215 ± 1.685 × (20/√40) = 215 ± 5.33 = (209.67, 220.33)
  • 95% CI: 215 ± 2.023 × (20/√40) = 215 ± 6.39 = (208.61, 221.39)
  • 99% CI: 215 ± 2.708 × (20/√40) = 215 ± 8.56 = (206.44, 223.56)

Effect of Sample Size (95% confidence):

  • n = 40: 215 ± 2.023 × (20/√40) = 215 ± 6.39 = (208.61, 221.39)
  • n = 100: 215 ± 1.984 × (20/√100) = 215 ± 3.97 = (211.03, 218.97)
  • n = 400: 215 ± 1.966 × (20/√400) = 215 ± 1.97 = (213.03, 216.97)

The interval width decreases as sample size increases and increases as confidence level increases.

One-Sided Confidence Intervals

While most confidence intervals are two-sided (with an upper and lower bound), one-sided intervals provide either a lower bound or an upper bound, but not both.

Lower One-Sided Confidence Interval

(Point Estimate - Critical Value × Standard Error, ∞)

Upper One-Sided Confidence Interval

(-∞, Point Estimate + Critical Value × Standard Error)

One-sided intervals are useful when only one direction is of interest, such as when we want to ensure a parameter exceeds or falls below a certain threshold.

Example 5: One-Sided Confidence Interval

A company claims its light bulbs last at least 1000 hours. A sample of 36 light bulbs has a mean lifetime of 1050 hours with a standard deviation of 120 hours. Construct a 95% lower confidence bound for the mean lifetime.

For a one-sided 95% confidence interval, t0.05, 35 = 1.69

Lower bound = 1050 - 1.69 × (120/√36) = 1050 - 1.69 × 20 = 1050 - 33.8 = 1016.2 hours

With 95% confidence, we can say that the mean lifetime of all light bulbs of this type is at least 1016.2 hours.

Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

Bootstrap methods provide a non-parametric approach to constructing confidence intervals without making assumptions about the underlying distribution of the data.

The basic bootstrap procedure involves:

  1. Taking repeated random samples (with replacement) from the original sample
  2. Calculating the statistic of interest for each bootstrap sample
  3. Using the distribution of bootstrap statistics to construct a confidence interval

Common Bootstrap Confidence Interval Methods

  • Percentile Method: Uses the percentiles of the bootstrap distribution
  • BCa (Bias-Corrected and Accelerated) Method: Adjusts for bias and skewness in the bootstrap distribution
  • Bootstrap-t Method: Uses studentized bootstrap statistics

Bootstrap methods are particularly useful when:

  • The sample size is small
  • The sampling distribution is unknown or non-normal
  • The parameter of interest is complex (e.g., median, correlation coefficient)
Example 6: Bootstrap Confidence Interval

A researcher wants to estimate the median income in a small community. The sample data doesn't follow a normal distribution, making traditional methods less reliable.

Using bootstrap methods:

  1. The researcher takes 1000 bootstrap samples from the original data.
  2. For each bootstrap sample, the median income is calculated.
  3. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of these 1000 bootstrap medians are used as the lower and upper bounds of a 95% confidence interval.

This approach provides a valid confidence interval without assuming normality or using complex formulas.

Applications of Confidence Intervals

Confidence intervals have numerous practical applications:

Research and Science

  • Reporting experimental results with measures of precision
  • Comparing treatments or interventions
  • Meta-analysis of multiple studies

Business and Economics

  • Estimating market share or customer satisfaction
  • Forecasting sales or revenue
  • Quality control in manufacturing

Public Health and Medicine

  • Estimating disease prevalence
  • Reporting treatment effectiveness
  • Pharmaceutical trials and drug development

Policy and Decision Making

  • Election polling
  • Environmental regulation
  • Public opinion research
Example 7: Practical Application of Confidence Intervals

A polling organization reports that 52% of voters support a candidate, with a margin of error of ±3% at 95% confidence.

This means the 95% confidence interval for the true proportion of voters supporting the candidate is (49%, 55%).

Since this interval includes 50%, we cannot conclude with 95% confidence that the candidate has majority support. This information helps campaign strategists make informed decisions about resource allocation and messaging.
