Descriptive Statistics

Organizing, summarizing, and presenting data

Introduction to Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics involves methods for organizing, summarizing, and presenting data in a meaningful way. Unlike inferential statistics, which aims to draw conclusions about a population based on a sample, descriptive statistics focuses solely on describing the main features of the collected data.

The main purposes of descriptive statistics are:

  • To organize raw data into a meaningful form
  • To summarize important characteristics of the data
  • To present the data in an easily interpretable way (tables, graphs, numerical measures)
  • To identify patterns, trends, and relationships within the data

Descriptive statistics can be classified into:

  1. Measures of Central Tendency: Values that represent the center or average of the data
  2. Measures of Dispersion: Values that describe how spread out the data is
  3. Measures of Shape: Descriptions of the data distribution's symmetry and peakedness
  4. Data Visualization: Graphical representations of the data

Types of Data

Before analyzing data, it's important to understand the different types of data:

Qualitative (Categorical) Data

Data that represents characteristics or qualities that can't be measured numerically.

  • Nominal Data: Categories with no natural order (e.g., eye color, gender, country)
  • Ordinal Data: Categories with a natural order (e.g., educational level, satisfaction ratings)

Quantitative (Numerical) Data

Data that represents quantities that can be measured numerically.

  • Discrete Data: Values that can only take specific values, typically counts (e.g., number of children, number of cars)
  • Continuous Data: Values that can take any value within a range (e.g., height, weight, temperature)
Example 1: Identifying Data Types

Classify each of the following variables by data type:

  • Blood type (A, B, AB, O) - Nominal (categories with no natural order)
  • Temperature in degrees Celsius - Continuous (can take any value within a range)
  • Satisfaction rating (1-5 stars) - Ordinal (categories with a natural order)
  • Number of siblings - Discrete (counts that take specific values)

Measures of Central Tendency

Measures of central tendency identify the center or "typical value" of a dataset, helping to summarize the data with a single value.

Mean (Arithmetic Average)

The mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of values:

Mean (μ or x̄) = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ)/n = Σxᵢ/n

The mean is sensitive to extreme values (outliers) and is most appropriate for symmetric distributions.

Median

The median is the middle value when the data is arranged in ascending or descending order:

  • For odd number of observations: the value at position (n+1)/2
  • For even number of observations: the average of values at positions n/2 and (n/2)+1

The median is not affected by extreme values and is suitable for skewed distributions.

Mode

The mode is the value that occurs most frequently in the dataset. A distribution can have:

  • Unimodal: One mode
  • Bimodal: Two modes
  • Multimodal: More than two modes
  • No mode: No value appears more than once

The mode is the only measure of central tendency applicable to nominal data.

Example 2: Calculating Measures of Central Tendency

Consider the dataset: 4, 7, 2, 8, 5, 3, 12, 6, 9

Mean: (4 + 7 + 2 + 8 + 5 + 3 + 12 + 6 + 9) / 9 = 56 / 9 = 6.22

Median: First arrange in order: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 12

Since there are 9 values (odd), the median is the 5th value: 6

Mode: No value appears more than once, so there is no mode.

Measures of Dispersion

Measures of dispersion describe how spread out the data is around the center.

Range

The range is the difference between the maximum and minimum values:

Range = Maximum value - Minimum value

While simple to calculate, the range only considers two extreme values and ignores the distribution of values in between.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The IQR is the range of the middle 50% of the data:

IQR = Q₃ - Q₁

Where:

  • Q₁ (First quartile): 25th percentile
  • Q₃ (Third quartile): 75th percentile

The IQR is not affected by extreme values and is useful for identifying outliers.

Variance

Variance measures the average squared deviation from the mean:

Population variance (σ²) = Σ(xᵢ - μ)²/N
Sample variance (s²) = Σ(xᵢ - x̄)²/(n-1)

The variance gives greater weight to values far from the mean but has the drawback of being in squared units.

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance, returning to the original units:

Population standard deviation (σ) = √(σ²)
Sample standard deviation (s) = √(s²)

The standard deviation is widely used as it provides a consistent way to identify unusual values and compare different datasets.

Example 3: Calculating Measures of Dispersion

Using the dataset: 4, 7, 2, 8, 5, 3, 12, 6, 9

Range: 12 - 2 = 10

Interquartile Range (IQR):

Arranged data: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 12

Q₁ (25th percentile): 3

Q₃ (75th percentile): 9

IQR = 9 - 3 = 6

Sample Variance:

Mean (x̄) = 6.22

Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² = (4-6.22)² + (7-6.22)² + ... + (9-6.22)² = 78.22

s² = 78.22/(9-1) = 9.78

Sample Standard Deviation:

s = √9.78 = 3.13

Measures of Shape

Skewness

Skewness measures the asymmetry of the data distribution:

  • Positive Skew (Right Skew): The distribution has a longer tail on the right, with most values concentrated on the left. Mean > Median > Mode.
  • Zero Skew (Symmetric): The distribution is symmetric. Mean = Median = Mode.
  • Negative Skew (Left Skew): The distribution has a longer tail on the left, with most values concentrated on the right. Mean < Median < Mode.

Kurtosis

Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" or peakedness of the distribution:

  • Leptokurtic (Positive Kurtosis): Distribution has heavier tails and a sharper peak than a normal distribution.
  • Mesokurtic (Zero Kurtosis): Distribution has the same tailedness as a normal distribution.
  • Platykurtic (Negative Kurtosis): Distribution has lighter tails and a flatter peak than a normal distribution.
Example 4: Interpreting Skewness and Kurtosis

Consider these scenarios:

1. Income Distribution: Often positively skewed because most people earn low to moderate incomes while a few earn very high incomes, pulling the mean higher than the median.

2. Exam Scores After Intensive Preparation: May be negatively skewed because most students perform well, but a few perform poorly, pulling the mean lower than the median.

3. Heights of Adult Humans: Approximately symmetric (zero skew) and follows a normal distribution (mesokurtic).

Data Visualization

Visual representations help identify patterns and trends that might not be apparent from numerical summaries alone.

For Categorical Data

  • Bar Chart: Displays frequencies or counts for different categories with rectangular bars.
  • Pie Chart: Shows the proportion of each category as a slice of a circle, suitable when representing parts of a whole.

For Numerical Data

  • Histogram: Displays frequency distributions by dividing data into intervals (bins) and showing the count in each bin.
  • Box Plot (Box-and-Whisker Plot): Shows the five-number summary (minimum, Q₁, median, Q₃, maximum) and identifies outliers.
  • Stem-and-Leaf Plot: Displays individual data values while showing the distribution shape.
  • Scatter Plot: Shows the relationship between two numerical variables.
Example 5: Creating a Frequency Table and Histogram

Consider these test scores: 65, 72, 83, 90, 67, 75, 78, 88, 92, 71, 69, 84, 76, 85, 79

Frequency Table:

Interval Frequency
60-69 3
70-79 6
80-89 4
90-99 2

A histogram would visually represent this frequency table, with intervals on the x-axis and frequencies on the y-axis, showing the distribution of test scores.

Z-Scores and Percentiles

Z-Scores (Standard Scores)

Z-scores measure how many standard deviations a data point is from the mean:

Z = (X - μ) / σ

Where:

  • X is the raw score
  • μ is the population mean
  • σ is the population standard deviation

Z-scores allow for standardized comparisons across different datasets. A z-score of 0 means the value equals the mean, positive z-scores are above the mean, and negative z-scores are below the mean.

Percentiles

A percentile indicates the percentage of values that fall at or below a given value:

  • The 50th percentile is the median
  • The 25th percentile is the first quartile (Q₁)
  • The 75th percentile is the third quartile (Q₃)

Percentiles are useful for understanding an individual's standing relative to a group.

Example 6: Calculating Z-Scores

In a class where the mean test score is 75 with a standard deviation of 8:

1. A student who scored 83 has a z-score of: (83 - 75) / 8 = 1

This student scored 1 standard deviation above the mean.

2. A student who scored 67 has a z-score of: (67 - 75) / 8 = -1

This student scored 1 standard deviation below the mean.

3. A student who scored 95 has a z-score of: (95 - 75) / 8 = 2.5

This student scored 2.5 standard deviations above the mean, which is quite high.

Applications of Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics has wide-ranging applications across various fields:

Business and Economics

  • Analyzing sales trends and customer demographics
  • Summarizing financial performance metrics
  • Monitoring quality control in manufacturing
  • Understanding market trends and consumer behavior

Social Sciences

  • Describing population demographics
  • Summarizing survey responses
  • Analyzing social trends and patterns
  • Comparing group characteristics

Health and Medicine

  • Summarizing patient health metrics
  • Describing disease prevalence and distribution
  • Analyzing treatment outcomes
  • Monitoring public health indicators

Education

  • Analyzing student performance
  • Comparing test scores across different groups
  • Evaluating program effectiveness
  • Identifying areas for improvement
Example 7: Real-World Application

A retail company analyzes its sales data for the past year and finds:

  • Mean daily sales: $12,500
  • Median daily sales: $11,800
  • Standard deviation: $3,200
  • Sales distribution: Positively skewed

Interpretation: The positive skew (mean > median) indicates there were several days with exceptionally high sales that pulled the mean upward. The standard deviation suggests moderate day-to-day variation in sales. The company might focus on understanding what factors contributed to those high-sales days and try to replicate those conditions.
