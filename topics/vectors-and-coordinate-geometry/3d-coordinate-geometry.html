Understanding geometric objects and relationships in three-dimensional space

Introduction to 3D Coordinate Geometry

Three-dimensional coordinate geometry extends the principles of 2D geometry into space, allowing us to describe and analyze points, lines, planes, and other geometric objects in three dimensions. This branch of mathematics provides the foundation for various fields, including computer graphics, engineering design, physics simulations, and more.

While we've covered coordinate systems and equations of lines and planes in previous sections, this page will focus on specific 3D geometric objects, their properties, and relationships in space.

Historical Context

The formal study of 3D geometry dates back to ancient Greece, but the analytical approach using coordinates was developed in the 17th century with René Descartes' work. The addition of the z-coordinate to extend the Cartesian plane into 3D space revolutionized how mathematicians could describe and analyze spatial relationships. Later, advances in vector calculus and differential geometry further enhanced our understanding of objects in three-dimensional space.

Points and Distances in 3D Space

Distance Between Points

The distance between two points P₁(x₁, y₁, z₁) and P₂(x₂, y₂, z₂) in 3D space is given by:

d(P₁, P₂) = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)² + (z₂ - z₁)²]

Midpoint Formula

The midpoint M of a line segment with endpoints P₁(x₁, y₁, z₁) and P₂(x₂, y₂, z₂) is:

M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2, (z₁ + z₂)/2)

Section Formula

The point P that divides the line segment from P₁(x₁, y₁, z₁) to P₂(x₂, y₂, z₂) in the ratio m:n is:

P = ((mx₂ + nx₁)/(m + n), (my₂ + ny₁)/(m + n), (mz₂ + nz₁)/(m + n))
Example 1: Distance and Midpoint

Consider the points A(3, -1, 4) and B(5, 2, -2).

Find the distance between A and B:

d(A, B) = √[(5 - 3)² + (2 - (-1))² + (-2 - 4)²]

d(A, B) = √[4 + 9 + 36] = √49 = 7 units

Find the midpoint of segment AB:

M = ((3 + 5)/2, (-1 + 2)/2, (4 + (-2))/2) = (4, 0.5, 1)

Find the point P that divides AB in the ratio 2:3:

P = ((2×5 + 3×3)/(2 + 3), (2×2 + 3×(-1))/(2 + 3), (2×(-2) + 3×4)/(2 + 3))

P = ((10 + 9)/5, (4 - 3)/5, (-4 + 12)/5) = (3.8, 0.2, 1.6)

Spheres

Definition and Equation

A sphere is the set of all points in 3D space that are at a fixed distance (the radius) from a fixed point (the center).

The standard equation of a sphere with center C(h, k, l) and radius r is:

(x - h)² + (y - k)² + (z - l)² = r²

If we expand this equation, we get the general form:

x² + y² + z² + Dx + Ey + Fz + G = 0

where D = -2h, E = -2k, F = -2l, and G = h² + k² + l² - r²

Properties of a Sphere

  • Surface area: A = 4πr²
  • Volume: V = (4/3)πr³
  • Every cross-section of a sphere by a plane is a circle
  • The intersection of a sphere and a plane is a circle
Example 2: Sphere Equation

Find the equation of a sphere with center at (2, -1, 3) and radius 4.

Solution:

Using the standard equation with h = 2, k = -1, l = 3, and r = 4:

(x - 2)² + (y - (-1))² + (z - 3)² = 4²

(x - 2)² + (y + 1)² + (z - 3)² = 16

Expanding:

x² - 4x + 4 + y² + 2y + 1 + z² - 6z + 9 = 16

x² + y² + z² - 4x + 2y - 6z + 14 - 16 = 0

x² + y² + z² - 4x + 2y - 6z - 2 = 0

Cylinders and Cones

Circular Cylinder

A circular cylinder with its axis parallel to the z-axis can be represented by the equation:

(x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²

where (h, k) is the center of the circular base in the xy-plane and r is the radius.

Elliptic Cylinder

An elliptic cylinder with its axis parallel to the z-axis has the equation:

((x - h)/a)² + ((y - k)/b)² = 1

where a and b are the semi-major and semi-minor axes of the elliptical base.

Circular Cone

A circular cone with its vertex at the origin and axis along the z-axis has the equation:

x² + y² = k²z²

where k is related to the angle between the axis and the generating line.

Example 3: Identifying Quadric Surfaces

Identify the type of quadric surface represented by:

a) x² + y² = 9

b) x² + y² = z²

Solution:

a) x² + y² = 9 or x² + y² = 3² represents a circular cylinder with radius 3 and axis along the z-axis. The equation doesn't involve z, meaning the surface extends indefinitely in the z-direction.

b) x² + y² = z² represents a circular cone with vertex at the origin and axis along the z-axis. Here, k = 1, meaning the cone has a 45° angle between its axis and generating line.

Quadric Surfaces

Quadric surfaces are 3D analogs of conic sections and are represented by second-degree equations in x, y, and z.

Ellipsoid

(x/a)² + (y/b)² + (z/c)² = 1

where a, b, and c are the semi-axes lengths along the x, y, and z directions, respectively.

Hyperboloid of One Sheet

(x/a)² + (y/b)² - (z/c)² = 1

Hyperboloid of Two Sheets

(x/a)² - (y/b)² - (z/c)² = 1

Elliptic Paraboloid

(x/a)² + (y/b)² = z/c

Hyperbolic Paraboloid

(x/a)² - (y/b)² = z/c
Example 4: Describing a Quadric Surface

Describe the quadric surface with equation x²/4 + y²/9 + z²/16 = 1.

Solution:

The equation can be rewritten as (x/2)² + (y/3)² + (z/4)² = 1, which is the standard form of an ellipsoid.

This ellipsoid has semi-axes of length 2 along the x-axis, 3 along the y-axis, and 4 along the z-axis.

The center of the ellipsoid is at the origin (0, 0, 0).

Transformations in 3D Space

Reflection

Reflections in 3D space can be across a plane. For example, reflection across the xy-plane transforms (x, y, z) to (x, y, -z).

Rotation

Rotations in 3D space are more complex than in 2D. They can be around any axis and are often represented using rotation matrices or quaternions.

For example, a rotation around the z-axis by angle θ transforms coordinates as:

x' = x·cos(θ) - y·sin(θ)
y' = x·sin(θ) + y·cos(θ)
z' = z

Translation

A translation by vector (a, b, c) transforms point (x, y, z) to (x+a, y+b, z+c).

Scaling

Scaling by factors (sx, sy, sz) transforms point (x, y, z) to (sx·x, sy·y, sz·z).

Example 5: 3D Transformations

Apply the following transformations to the point P(3, 2, 1):

a) Reflection across the xz-plane

b) Rotation of 90° around the z-axis

c) Translation by vector (1, -2, 3)

Solution:

a) Reflection across the xz-plane changes the y-coordinate to its negative:

P' = (3, -2, 1)

b) Rotation of 90° around the z-axis (θ = π/2):

x' = 3·cos(π/2) - 2·sin(π/2) = 3·0 - 2·1 = -2

y' = 3·sin(π/2) + 2·cos(π/2) = 3·1 + 2·0 = 3

z' = 1

So P' = (-2, 3, 1)

c) Translation by vector (1, -2, 3):

P' = (3+1, 2+(-2), 1+3) = (4, 0, 4)

Applications in Computer Graphics

3D Modeling

3D coordinate geometry is fundamental to computer-aided design (CAD) and computer graphics. Objects are represented as collections of points, lines, and surfaces in 3D space.

Rendering and Projection

To display 3D objects on 2D screens, projection techniques are used. Common projections include:

  • Orthographic projection: Preserves parallel lines and relative dimensions
  • Perspective projection: Creates realistic depth perception with objects farther away appearing smaller

Collision Detection

In games and simulations, 3D geometry is used to determine when objects intersect or collide. This involves calculating distances between various geometric shapes.

Example 6: Perspective Projection

A perspective projection with the camera at the origin looking down the positive z-axis can be represented by the transformation:

x' = (d·x)/z

y' = (d·y)/z

where d is the distance from the camera to the projection plane.

If a point P(2, 3, 5) is viewed with d = 10, then its projection on the screen would be:

x' = (10·2)/5 = 4

y' = (10·3)/5 = 6

So the projection of P on the screen would be at coordinates (4, 6).
