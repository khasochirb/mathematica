Equations of Lines & Planes

Understanding the mathematical representation of lines and planes in space

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Vectors and Coordinate Geometry » Equations of Lines & Planes

Introduction to Lines and Planes

Lines and planes are fundamental geometric objects that can be described algebraically using equations. These equations allow us to analyze their properties, determine their positions in space, and calculate their intersections. Understanding the various forms of these equations is essential for solving problems in three-dimensional geometry, computer graphics, robotics, and many scientific fields.

This page explores the different ways to represent lines and planes mathematically, with a focus on vector equations and parametric representations.

Historical Context

The study of lines and planes has ancient origins, but the modern algebraic treatment emerged with René Descartes's development of coordinate geometry in the 17th century. The vector approach to representing lines and planes became prominent in the 19th and early 20th centuries, particularly through the work of mathematicians like Hermann Grassmann, William Rowan Hamilton, and Josiah Willard Gibbs. This vector approach has proven especially powerful for dealing with objects in three-dimensional space and beyond.

Lines in Two-Dimensional Space

Different Forms of Line Equations in 2D

A line in two-dimensional space can be represented in several ways:

1. Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b

where m is the slope of the line and b is the y-intercept.

2. Point-Slope Form

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

where (x₁, y₁) is a point on the line and m is the slope.

3. Two-Point Form

y - y₁ = ((y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁))(x - x₁)

where (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) are two distinct points on the line.

4. General Form (Standard Form)

Ax + By + C = 0

where A, B, and C are constants and A and B are not both zero.

5. Intercept Form

x/a + y/b = 1

where a is the x-intercept and b is the y-intercept.

Example 1: Converting Between Line Representations

Consider a line passing through the points (1, 3) and (4, 9).

Find the line equation in:

a) Two-point form:

y - 3 = ((9 - 3)/(4 - 1))(x - 1)

y - 3 = (6/3)(x - 1)

y - 3 = 2(x - 1)

y - 3 = 2x - 2

y = 2x + 1

b) General form:

From y = 2x + 1, we get:

2x - y + 1 = 0

c) Intercept form:

If y = 0: 0 = 2x + 1, so x = -1/2

If x = 0: y = 2(0) + 1 = 1

Therefore: x/(-1/2) + y/1 = 1

-2x + y = 1

Or: x/(-1/2) + y/1 = 1

Lines in Three-Dimensional Space

In three-dimensional space, a line cannot be represented by a single equation. Instead, we use either parametric equations or vector equations.

1. Parametric Equations of a Line

x = x₀ + at
y = y₀ + bt
z = z₀ + ct

where (x₀, y₀, z₀) is a point on the line, (a, b, c) is a direction vector of the line, and t is the parameter.

2. Vector Equation of a Line

r = r₀ + tv

where r₀ is the position vector of a point on the line, v is a direction vector of the line, and t is the parameter.

3. Symmetric Equations of a Line

(x - x₀)/a = (y - y₀)/b = (z - z₀)/c

where (x₀, y₀, z₀) is a point on the line and (a, b, c) is a direction vector of the line.

4. Line as the Intersection of Two Planes

A line in 3D space can also be represented as the intersection of two planes:

A₁x + B₁y + C₁z + D₁ = 0
A₂x + B₂y + C₂z + D₂ = 0
Example 2: Representing a 3D Line

Find the vector, parametric, and symmetric equations of the line passing through the points P(1, 2, 3) and Q(4, 0, 5).

Solution:

First, we find the direction vector PQ:

PQ = Q - P = (4, 0, 5) - (1, 2, 3) = (3, -2, 2)

Vector equation:

r = (1, 2, 3) + t(3, -2, 2)

Parametric equations:

x = 1 + 3t

y = 2 - 2t

z = 3 + 2t

Symmetric equations:

(x - 1)/3 = (y - 2)/(-2) = (z - 3)/2

or: (x - 1)/3 = -(y - 2)/2 = (z - 3)/2

Planes in Three-Dimensional Space

1. General Form of a Plane Equation

Ax + By + Cz + D = 0

where A, B, C, and D are constants, and (A, B, C) is the normal vector to the plane.

2. Vector Equation of a Plane

n·(r - r₀) = 0

where n is the normal vector to the plane, r₀ is the position vector of a point on the plane, and r is the position vector of any point on the plane.

3. Intercept Form of a Plane

x/a + y/b + z/c = 1

where a, b, and c are the x, y, and z-intercepts of the plane, respectively.

4. Point-Normal Form of a Plane

A(x - x₀) + B(y - y₀) + C(z - z₀) = 0

where (x₀, y₀, z₀) is a point on the plane and (A, B, C) is the normal vector to the plane.

Example 3: Finding the Equation of a Plane

Find the equation of the plane passing through the point P(2, -1, 3) with normal vector n = (4, 5, -2).

Solution:

Using the point-normal form:

4(x - 2) + 5(y - (-1)) + (-2)(z - 3) = 0

4x - 8 + 5y + 5 - 2z + 6 = 0

4x + 5y - 2z + 3 = 0

This is the equation of the plane in general form with A = 4, B = 5, C = -2, and D = 3.

Distances and Angles

Distance from a Point to a Line

In 2D: If a line has the equation Ax + By + C = 0 and the point is (x₀, y₀), then the distance is:

d = |Ax₀ + By₀ + C| / √(A² + B²)

In 3D: If a line passes through point P₀ with direction vector v, and the point is P, then the distance is:

d = |P₀P × v| / |v|

where × denotes the cross product.

Distance from a Point to a Plane

If a plane has the equation Ax + By + Cz + D = 0 and the point is (x₀, y₀, z₀), then the distance is:

d = |Ax₀ + By₀ + Cz₀ + D| / √(A² + B² + C²)

Angle Between Two Lines

If v₁ and v₂ are the direction vectors of the lines, then the angle between them is:

cos(θ) = |v₁·v₂| / (|v₁||v₂|)

Angle Between Two Planes

If n₁ and n₂ are the normal vectors of the planes, then the angle between them is:

cos(θ) = |n₁·n₂| / (|n₁||n₂|)

Angle Between a Line and a Plane

If v is the direction vector of the line and n is the normal vector of the plane, then the angle between them is:

sin(θ) = |v·n| / (|v||n|)
Example 4: Distance Calculations

Find the distance from the point P(3, 1, 2) to the plane 2x - y + 2z - 4 = 0.

Solution:

Using the formula for the distance from a point to a plane:

d = |A(x₀) + B(y₀) + C(z₀) + D| / √(A² + B² + C²)

d = |2(3) - 1(1) + 2(2) - 4| / √(2² + (-1)² + 2²)

d = |6 - 1 + 4 - 4| / √(4 + 1 + 4)

d = |5| / √9 = 5/3 ≈ 1.67 units

Intersection of Lines and Planes

Intersection of Two Lines in 2D

For two lines with equations A₁x + B₁y + C₁ = 0 and A₂x + B₂y + C₂ = 0, the intersection point is found by solving the system of these two equations.

Intersection of a Line and a Plane

If a line has parametric equations:

x = x₀ + at
y = y₀ + bt
z = z₀ + ct

And a plane has the equation Ax + By + Cz + D = 0, then to find the intersection:

  1. Substitute the parametric equations into the plane equation
  2. Solve for parameter t
  3. Substitute t back into the parametric equations to find the intersection point

Intersection of Two Planes

The intersection of two planes is a line. If the planes have equations:

A₁x + B₁y + C₁z + D₁ = 0
A₂x + B₂y + C₂z + D₂ = 0

The direction vector of the line of intersection is the cross product of the normal vectors:

v = (A₁, B₁, C₁) × (A₂, B₂, C₂)

To find a point on the line, we solve the system of equations together with an additional constraint (e.g., setting one of the coordinates to zero).

Intersection of Three Planes

The intersection of three planes is typically a point, found by solving the system of three equations. However, if the planes are not independent, they might intersect in a line or even a plane, or they might not have a common intersection at all.

Example 5: Finding the Intersection of a Line and a Plane

Find the intersection of the line r = (1, 2, 3) + t(2, 0, 1) with the plane 2x + 3y - z + 4 = 0.

Solution:

The parametric equations of the line are:

x = 1 + 2t

y = 2

z = 3 + t

Substituting into the plane equation:

2(1 + 2t) + 3(2) - (3 + t) + 4 = 0

2 + 4t + 6 - 3 - t + 4 = 0

9 + 3t = 0

t = -3

Now we substitute t = -3 back into the parametric equations:

x = 1 + 2(-3) = 1 - 6 = -5

y = 2

z = 3 + (-3) = 0

Therefore, the intersection point is (-5, 2, 0).

Applications of Lines and Planes

Computer Graphics and Computer Vision

  • Ray tracing for rendering three-dimensional scenes
  • Camera calibration and stereo vision
  • Object detection and recognition

Robotics

  • Path planning and collision detection
  • Robotic arm movement along specified trajectories
  • 3D mapping and navigation

Physics and Engineering

  • Describing motion of objects along straight paths
  • Defining boundaries and surfaces in structural analysis
  • Analyzing reflections and refractions of light
Example 6: Reflection of a Ray

A light ray with direction vector v = (1, -1, 2) hits a reflective surface with normal vector n = (0, 0, 1). Find the direction vector of the reflected ray.

Solution:

We need to normalize both vectors:

|v| = √(1² + (-1)² + 2²) = √(1 + 1 + 4) = √6

v̂ = v/|v| = (1, -1, 2)/√6 ≈ (0.41, -0.41, 0.82)

|n| = √(0² + 0² + 1²) = 1

n̂ = n/|n| = (0, 0, 1)

The reflected ray direction is given by:

r = v̂ - 2(v̂·n̂)n̂

v̂·n̂ = (0.41 × 0) + (-0.41 × 0) + (0.82 × 1) = 0.82

r = (0.41, -0.41, 0.82) - 2(0.82)(0, 0, 1)

r = (0.41, -0.41, 0.82) - (0, 0, 1.64)

r = (0.41, -0.41, -0.82)

Therefore, the direction vector of the reflected ray is approximately (0.41, -0.41, -0.82), which has the same x and y components as the incident ray but the opposite z component.
