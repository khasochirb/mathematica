Law of Sines & Cosines

Solving triangles using powerful trigonometric relationships

Introduction to Triangle Trigonometry

While early trigonometry focused on right triangles, the Law of Sines and Law of Cosines extend trigonometric relationships to any triangle. These powerful laws allow us to solve triangles completely (find all sides and angles) when only partial information is available.

Consider a triangle with:

  • Sides: a, b, and c
  • Angles: A (opposite side a), B (opposite side b), and C (opposite side c)

A triangle is completely determined if we know:

  • Three sides (SSS): a, b, and c
  • Two sides and the included angle (SAS): a, b, and C
  • Two angles and the included side (ASA): A, B, and c
  • Two angles and a non-included side (AAS): A, B, and a

Remember that in any triangle, the sum of all angles is 180° (or π radians):

A + B + C = 180° (or π radians)
Example 1: Finding the Third Angle

In triangle ABC, if angle A = 42° and angle B = 57°, find angle C.

Using the property that angles in a triangle sum to 180°:

C = 180° - A - B = 180° - 42° - 57° = 81°

The Law of Sines

The Law of Sines states that in any triangle, the ratio of the length of a side to the sine of the opposite angle is constant for all three sides and angles.

a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) = c/sin(C)

This can also be written as:

sin(A)/a = sin(B)/b = sin(C)/c

The Law of Sines is particularly useful for solving triangles in the following cases:

  • ASA (Angle-Side-Angle): When you know two angles and any side
  • AAS (Angle-Angle-Side): When you know two angles and a non-included side
  • SSA (Side-Side-Angle): When you know two sides and an angle opposite one of them (but beware the ambiguous case)
Example 2: ASA Case

In triangle ABC, if angle A = 65°, angle B = 45°, and side c = 12 cm, find the lengths of sides a and b.

Step 1: Find the third angle using the angle sum property.

C = 180° - A - B = 180° - 65° - 45° = 70°

Step 2: Use the Law of Sines to find sides a and b.

a/sin(A) = c/sin(C)

a = c × sin(A)/sin(C) = 12 × sin(65°)/sin(70°) = 12 × 0.9063/0.9397 = 11.57 cm

b/sin(B) = c/sin(C)

b = c × sin(B)/sin(C) = 12 × sin(45°)/sin(70°) = 12 × 0.7071/0.9397 = 9.03 cm

The Ambiguous Case (SSA)

When using the Law of Sines with two sides and an angle opposite one of them (SSA), multiple solutions may exist. This is known as the ambiguous case, and it can result in:

  • No solution (impossible triangle)
  • One solution (unique triangle)
  • Two solutions (two possible triangles)

To determine the number of solutions, compare the known side (a) opposite the known angle (A) with the height h = b·sin(C):

  • If a > b, there is exactly 1 solution
  • If a = b·sin(A), there is exactly 1 solution (right triangle)
  • If b·sin(A) < a < b, there are 2 solutions
  • If a < b·sin(A), there are 0 solutions (impossible triangle)
Example 3: The Ambiguous Case

In triangle ABC, if angle A = 30°, side a = 8 cm, and side b = 12 cm, determine the number of possible triangles and find all solutions.

Step 1: Check for the ambiguous case.

Calculate h = b·sin(A) = 12·sin(30°) = 12·0.5 = 6 cm

Since a = 8 cm and b = 12 cm, we have b·sin(A) < a < b (6 < 8 < 12)

This means there are two possible triangles.

Step 2: Find angle B using the Law of Sines.

sin(B)/b = sin(A)/a

sin(B) = b·sin(A)/a = 12·sin(30°)/8 = 12·0.5/8 = 0.75

This gives us B₁ = sin⁻¹(0.75) ≈ 48.6° (acute angle)

But since sin(B) = sin(180° - B), we also have B₂ = 180° - 48.6° = 131.4° (obtuse angle)

Step 3: Find the corresponding angles C and sides c for each case.

For the first triangle (B₁ = 48.6°):

C₁ = 180° - A - B₁ = 180° - 30° - 48.6° = 101.4°

c₁/sin(C₁) = a/sin(A)

c₁ = a·sin(C₁)/sin(A) = 8·sin(101.4°)/sin(30°) = 8·0.9806/0.5 = 15.69 cm

For the second triangle (B₂ = 131.4°):

C₂ = 180° - A - B₂ = 180° - 30° - 131.4° = 18.6°

c₂/sin(C₂) = a/sin(A)

c₂ = a·sin(C₂)/sin(A) = 8·sin(18.6°)/sin(30°) = 8·0.3189/0.5 = 5.10 cm

Answer: There are two possible triangles:

Triangle 1: A = 30°, B₁ = 48.6°, C₁ = 101.4°, a = 8 cm, b = 12 cm, c₁ = 15.69 cm

Triangle 2: A = 30°, B₂ = 131.4°, C₂ = 18.6°, a = 8 cm, b = 12 cm, c₂ = 5.10 cm

The Law of Cosines

The Law of Cosines relates the square of any side of a triangle to the sum of the squares of the other two sides minus twice their product times the cosine of the included angle.

a² = b² + c² - 2bc·cos(A)
b² = a² + c² - 2ac·cos(B)
c² = a² + b² - 2ab·cos(C)

The Law of Cosines is a generalization of the Pythagorean theorem. In a right triangle with C = 90°, cos(C) = 0, so c² = a² + b².

The Law of Cosines is particularly useful for solving triangles in the following cases:

  • SSS (Side-Side-Side): When you know all three sides
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side): When you know two sides and the included angle

The Law of Cosines can also be rearranged to find angles:

cos(A) = (b² + c² - a²) / (2bc)
cos(B) = (a² + c² - b²) / (2ac)
cos(C) = (a² + b² - c²) / (2ab)
Example 4: SSS Case

In triangle ABC, if a = 5 cm, b = 7 cm, and c = 8 cm, find all three angles.

Step 1: Use the Law of Cosines to find angle A.

cos(A) = (b² + c² - a²) / (2bc) = (7² + 8² - 5²) / (2 × 7 × 8)

cos(A) = (49 + 64 - 25) / 112 = 88 / 112 = 0.7857

A = cos⁻¹(0.7857) ≈ 38.3°

Step 2: Find angle B using the Law of Cosines.

cos(B) = (a² + c² - b²) / (2ac) = (5² + 8² - 7²) / (2 × 5 × 8)

cos(B) = (25 + 64 - 49) / 80 = 40 / 80 = 0.5

B = cos⁻¹(0.5) = 60°

Step 3: Find angle C using the Law of Cosines or the angle sum property.

Using the angle sum property: C = 180° - A - B = 180° - 38.3° - 60° = 81.7°

Alternatively, using the Law of Cosines:

cos(C) = (a² + b² - c²) / (2ab) = (25 + 49 - 64) / (2 × 5 × 7) = 10 / 70 = 0.1429

C = cos⁻¹(0.1429) ≈ 81.8° (slight rounding difference)

Area of a Triangle

The Law of Sines and the Law of Cosines provide various formulas for calculating the area of a triangle:

Area Using Base and Height

Area = (1/2) × base × height

Area Using Two Sides and the Included Angle (SAS Formula)

Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin(C)

Heron's Formula (Using Three Sides)

Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

where s = (a + b + c)/2 is the semi-perimeter

Area Using the Law of Sines

Area = (1/2) × ab × sin(C) = (1/2) × bc × sin(A) = (1/2) × ac × sin(B)
Example 5: Area of a Triangle

Find the area of a triangle with sides a = 6 cm, b = 8 cm, and angle C = 30°.

Using the SAS formula:

Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin(C) = (1/2) × 6 × 8 × sin(30°)

Area = (1/2) × 6 × 8 × 0.5 = 12 cm²

To use Heron's formula, we first need the third side c:

Using the Law of Cosines: c² = a² + b² - 2ab·cos(C)

c² = 6² + 8² - 2 × 6 × 8 × cos(30°)

c² = 36 + 64 - 96 × 0.866 = 100 - 83.14 = 16.86

c = √16.86 ≈ 4.11 cm

Using Heron's formula:

s = (a + b + c)/2 = (6 + 8 + 4.11)/2 = 9.055 cm

Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

Area = √[9.055 × (9.055-6) × (9.055-8) × (9.055-4.11)]

Area = √[9.055 × 3.055 × 1.055 × 4.945]

Area = √[144.2] ≈ 12 cm²

Applications of the Laws of Sines and Cosines

The Laws of Sines and Cosines have numerous real-world applications:

Navigation and Surveying

  • Finding distances between inaccessible points
  • Determining position using triangulation
  • Measuring heights of structures

Physics and Engineering

  • Vector resolution and addition
  • Analyzing forces in structures
  • Calculating work done by a force

Astronomy

  • Measuring distances to celestial objects
  • Determining orbital parameters
  • Predicting cosmic events
Example 6: Applied Problem

Two fire lookout stations are 7 km apart along an east-west line. The first station spots a fire at an angle of 58° east of north. The second station spots the same fire at an angle of 62° west of north. How far is the fire from each station?

Step 1: Set up a triangle with the two stations and the fire as vertices.

Let's call the first station A, the second station B, and the fire C.

We know the distance AB = 7 km.

Angle A (east of north) is 90° - 58° = 32°.

Angle B (west of north) is 90° - 62° = 28°.

Step 2: Find the third angle C.

C = 180° - A - B = 180° - 32° - 28° = 120°

Step 3: Use the Law of Sines to find the distances.

AC/sin(B) = AB/sin(C)

AC = AB × sin(B)/sin(C) = 7 × sin(28°)/sin(120°) = 7 × 0.4695/0.866 = 3.8 km

BC/sin(A) = AB/sin(C)

BC = AB × sin(A)/sin(C) = 7 × sin(32°)/sin(120°) = 7 × 0.5299/0.866 = 4.3 km

Answer: The fire is approximately 3.8 km from the first station and 4.3 km from the second station.
