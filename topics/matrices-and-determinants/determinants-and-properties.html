Determinants & Properties

Understanding determinants and their fundamental properties in linear algebra

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Matrices and Determinants » Determinants & Properties

Introduction to Determinants

The determinant is a scalar value that can be computed from a square matrix. It provides valuable information about the matrix and has numerous applications in linear algebra and beyond. Determinants help us understand whether a matrix is invertible, find the area or volume of geometric transformations, and solve systems of linear equations.

Notation and Basic Concept

For a square matrix A, the determinant is denoted as det(A) or |A|. The determinant encodes information about how the linear transformation represented by the matrix changes volume.

Key concepts about determinants:

  • Determinants are defined only for square matrices.
  • The determinant of a matrix is zero if and only if the matrix is singular (not invertible).
  • The determinant of a product equals the product of the determinants: det(AB) = det(A) × det(B).
  • The determinant of the transpose equals the determinant of the original matrix: det(AT) = det(A).

Calculating Determinants

Determinant of a 2×2 Matrix

For a 2×2 matrix A = [[a, b], [c, d]], the determinant is calculated as:

det(A) = ad - bc
Example: Determinant of a 2×2 Matrix

For the matrix:

A = [
  3  5
  2  4
]

The determinant is:

det(A) = 3×4 - 5×2 = 12 - 10 = 2

Determinant of a 3×3 Matrix

For a 3×3 matrix, the determinant can be calculated using various methods, including the Sarrus rule or expansion by cofactors.

Using the Sarrus rule:

For A = [[a, b, c], [d, e, f], [g, h, i]]
det(A) = a(ei - fh) - b(di - fg) + c(dh - eg)
Example: Determinant of a 3×3 Matrix

For the matrix:

A = [
  2  1  3
  4  2  1
  0  3  5
]

The determinant is:

det(A) = 2(2×5 - 1×3) - 1(4×5 - 1×0) + 3(4×3 - 2×0)
= 2(10 - 3) - 1(20 - 0) + 3(12 - 0)
= 2(7) - 1(20) + 3(12)
= 14 - 20 + 36
= 30

Determinant of Larger Matrices

For matrices of size n×n where n > 3, the determinant is typically calculated by expansion along a row or column, using cofactors. The formula for expanding along the first row is:

det(A) = a11C11 + a12C12 + ... + a1nC1n

where Cij is the cofactor of element aij, defined as:

Cij = (-1)i+j × Mij

and Mij is the minor of aij, which is the determinant of the (n-1)×(n-1) matrix obtained by deleting the i-th row and j-th column of A.

Properties of Determinants

Basic Properties

  1. Determinant of the Identity Matrix: det(I) = 1
  2. Determinant of Transpose: det(AT) = det(A)
  3. Determinant of Product: det(AB) = det(A) × det(B)
  4. Determinant of Scalar Multiplication: det(kA) = kn × det(A), where A is an n×n matrix
  5. Determinant of Inverse: det(A-1) = 1/det(A) for any invertible matrix A

Effect of Row/Column Operations

  1. Row/Column Swap: If A' is obtained from A by swapping two rows or two columns, then det(A') = -det(A)
  2. Row/Column Scaling: If A' is obtained from A by multiplying a row or column by a scalar k, then det(A') = k × det(A)
  3. Row/Column Addition: If A' is obtained from A by adding a multiple of one row/column to another, then det(A') = det(A)
Example: Effect of Row Operations

For the matrix:

A = [
  2  1
  3  4
]

det(A) = 2×4 - 1×3 = 8 - 3 = 5

1. Row Swap: Swapping the two rows of A:

A' = [
  3  4
  2  1
]

det(A') = 3×1 - 4×2 = 3 - 8 = -5 = -det(A)

2. Row Scaling: Multiplying the first row of A by 2:

A'' = [
  4  2
  3  4
]

det(A'') = 4×4 - 2×3 = 16 - 6 = 10 = 2×det(A)

Special Cases and Properties

  • Singular Matrix: A matrix is singular (not invertible) if and only if its determinant is zero.
  • Triangular Matrices: The determinant of a triangular matrix (upper or lower) equals the product of its diagonal entries.
  • Similar Matrices: If matrices A and B are similar (i.e., B = P-1AP for some invertible matrix P), then det(A) = det(B).
  • Block Matrices: For a block diagonal matrix, the determinant equals the product of the determinants of the diagonal blocks.
Example: Determinant of a Triangular Matrix

For the upper triangular matrix:

U = [
  3  1  4
  0  2  5
  0  0  6
]

The determinant is simply the product of the diagonal entries:

det(U) = 3 × 2 × 6 = 36

Geometric Interpretation of Determinants

Area and Volume

The absolute value of the determinant of a matrix represents a scaling factor for areas (2×2 matrices) or volumes (3×3 matrices) under the transformation represented by the matrix.

  • For a 2×2 matrix, |det(A)| gives the area of the parallelogram formed by the transformed unit vectors.
  • For a 3×3 matrix, |det(A)| gives the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the transformed unit vectors.
Example: Area Interpretation

Consider the 2×2 matrix:

A = [
  3  1
  2  4
]

The determinant is det(A) = 3×4 - 1×2 = 12 - 2 = 10

This means that the area of the parallelogram formed by the vectors (3,2) and (1,4) is 10 square units, which is 10 times the area of the unit square formed by the standard basis vectors.

Orientation

The sign of the determinant indicates whether the orientation of space is preserved or reversed by the transformation:

  • If det(A) > 0, the orientation is preserved.
  • If det(A) < 0, the orientation is reversed.
  • If det(A) = 0, the transformation collapses space to a lower dimension.
Example: Orientation

For the matrix:

A = [
  0  1
  1  0
]

det(A) = 0×0 - 1×1 = -1

The negative determinant indicates that this transformation (which swaps the x and y coordinates) reverses orientation.

Applications of Determinants

Solving Systems of Linear Equations: Cramer's Rule

Cramer's rule provides a formula for the solution of a system of linear equations in terms of determinants. For a system Ax = b, where A is an n×n invertible matrix, the solution is given by:

xi = det(Ai)/det(A)

where Ai is the matrix formed by replacing the i-th column of A with the column vector b.

Example: Cramer's Rule

Consider the system:

2x + y = 5
3x + 4y = 11

In matrix form, this is:

[
  2  1
  3  4
] [
  x
  y
] = [
  5
  11
]

Matrix A = [[2, 1], [3, 4]], so det(A) = 2×4 - 1×3 = 5

For x, replace the first column with b:

A1 = [
  5  1
  11  4
]

det(A1) = 5×4 - 1×11 = 20 - 11 = 9

Thus, x = det(A1)/det(A) = 9/5

For y, replace the second column with b:

A2 = [
  2  5
  3  11
]

det(A2) = 2×11 - 5×3 = 22 - 15 = 7

Thus, y = det(A2)/det(A) = 7/5

The solution is x = 9/5 and y = 7/5.

Finding the Inverse of a Matrix

The inverse of a square matrix A can be expressed using the adjugate matrix and the determinant:

A-1 = adj(A)/det(A)

where adj(A) is the adjugate matrix, whose (i,j)-entry is the cofactor of the (j,i)-entry of A.

Eigenvalues and Characteristic Polynomial

The eigenvalues of a matrix A are the roots of its characteristic polynomial, which is defined as:

p(λ) = det(A - λI)

where I is the identity matrix of the same size as A.

Change of Variables in Multiple Integrals

When performing a change of variables in multiple integrals, the Jacobian determinant appears as a scaling factor:

∫∫ f(x,y) dx dy = ∫∫ f(g(u,v), h(u,v)) |J| du dv

where J is the Jacobian matrix of the transformation, and |J| is its determinant.
