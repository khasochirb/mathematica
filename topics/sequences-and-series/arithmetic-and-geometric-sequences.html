Arithmetic & Geometric Sequences

Understanding patterns and progressions in mathematical sequences

Introduction to Sequences

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers or objects that follow a specific pattern. In mathematics, sequences are typically represented as a list of numbers a₁, a₂, a₃, ..., aₙ, ... where each number in the sequence is called a term. The subscript denotes the position of the term in the sequence.

Sequences can be defined in several ways:

  • Explicit Formula: Define each term directly as a function of its position, e.g., aₙ = 2n + 1
  • Recursive Formula: Define each term in relation to previous terms, e.g., aₙ = aₙ₋₁ + 3 with a₁ = 5
  • Pattern Description: Define a sequence by describing the pattern, e.g., "add 3 to each term to get the next term"

Two of the most important and commonly encountered types of sequences are arithmetic sequences and geometric sequences, which we will explore in detail in this lesson.

Arithmetic Sequences

Definition and Properties

An arithmetic sequence (also called an arithmetic progression) is a sequence where the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is called the common difference, typically denoted by d.

If we have an arithmetic sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, ..., aₙ, ... then:

a₂ - a₁ = a₃ - a₂ = ... = aₙ - aₙ₋₁ = d

Given the first term a₁ and the common difference d, we can find any term in the sequence using the explicit formula:

aₙ = a₁ + (n - 1)d
Example 1: Finding Terms in an Arithmetic Sequence

For the arithmetic sequence with a₁ = 5 and d = 3, find a₁₀.

Using the formula aₙ = a₁ + (n - 1)d:

a₁₀ = 5 + (10 - 1) × 3 = 5 + 9 × 3 = 5 + 27 = 32

Therefore, the 10th term of the sequence is 32.

Example 2: Finding the Common Difference

For the arithmetic sequence 7, 11, 15, 19, ..., what is the common difference and the 15th term?

First, let's find the common difference:

d = a₂ - a₁ = 11 - 7 = 4

Now we can find the 15th term:

a₁₅ = a₁ + (15 - 1)d = 7 + 14 × 4 = 7 + 56 = 63

Arithmetic Means

Inserting arithmetic means between two numbers means creating an arithmetic sequence between those numbers. If we want to insert m arithmetic means between a and b, we'll have a sequence of m+2 terms (including a and b) with a common difference d = (b-a)/(m+1).

Example 3: Inserting Arithmetic Means

Insert 4 arithmetic means between 3 and 23.

We need to find the common difference d first:

d = (23 - 3)/(4 + 1) = 20/5 = 4

Now we can calculate the 4 means:

a₁ = 3

a₂ = 3 + 4 = 7

a₃ = 7 + 4 = 11

a₄ = 11 + 4 = 15

a₅ = 15 + 4 = 19

a₆ = 19 + 4 = 23

Therefore, the 4 arithmetic means are 7, 11, 15, and 19.

Sum of an Arithmetic Sequence

The sum of the first n terms of an arithmetic sequence, also known as an arithmetic series, can be calculated using the formula:

Sn = n/2[2a₁ + (n - 1)d] = n/2(a₁ + aₙ)

where a₁ is the first term, d is the common difference, and aₙ is the nth term.

Example 4: Finding the Sum of an Arithmetic Series

Find the sum of the first 20 terms of the arithmetic sequence 3, 7, 11, 15, ...

First, we identify a₁ = 3 and d = 4.

Using the formula aₙ = a₁ + (n - 1)d, we find:

a₂₀ = 3 + (20 - 1) × 4 = 3 + 19 × 4 = 3 + 76 = 79

Now we can use the formula for the sum:

S₂₀ = 20/2 × (3 + 79) = 10 × 82 = 820

Therefore, the sum of the first 20 terms is 820.

Geometric Sequences

Definition and Properties

A geometric sequence (also called a geometric progression) is a sequence where the ratio between consecutive terms is constant. This constant ratio is called the common ratio, typically denoted by r.

If we have a geometric sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, ..., aₙ, ... then:

a₂/a₁ = a₃/a₂ = ... = aₙ/aₙ₋₁ = r

Given the first term a₁ and the common ratio r, we can find any term in the sequence using the explicit formula:

aₙ = a₁ × r^(n-1)
Example 5: Finding Terms in a Geometric Sequence

For the geometric sequence with a₁ = 3 and r = 2, find a₆.

Using the formula aₙ = a₁ × r^(n-1):

a₆ = 3 × 2^(6-1) = 3 × 2^5 = 3 × 32 = 96

Therefore, the 6th term of the sequence is 96.

Example 6: Finding the Common Ratio

For the geometric sequence 5, 15, 45, 135, ..., what is the common ratio and the 8th term?

First, let's find the common ratio:

r = a₂/a₁ = 15/5 = 3

Now we can find the 8th term:

a₈ = a₁ × r^(8-1) = 5 × 3^7 = 5 × 2187 = 10,935

Geometric Means

Inserting geometric means between two numbers means creating a geometric sequence between those numbers. If we want to insert m geometric means between a and b, we'll have a sequence of m+2 terms (including a and b) with a common ratio r = (b/a)^(1/(m+1)).

Example 7: Inserting Geometric Means

Insert 2 geometric means between 2 and 54.

We need to find the common ratio r first:

r = (54/2)^(1/(2+1)) = 27^(1/3) = 3

Now we can calculate the 2 means:

a₁ = 2

a₂ = 2 × 3 = 6

a₃ = 6 × 3 = 18

a₄ = 18 × 3 = 54

Therefore, the 2 geometric means are 6 and 18.

Sum of a Geometric Sequence

The sum of the first n terms of a geometric sequence, also known as a geometric series, can be calculated using the formula:

Sn = a₁(1 - r^n)/(1 - r) (for r ≠ 1)

If r = 1, the sequence is constant (all terms are equal to a₁) and the sum is simply Sn = n·a₁.

Example 8: Finding the Sum of a Geometric Series

Find the sum of the first 6 terms of the geometric sequence 4, 12, 36, 108, ...

First, we identify a₁ = 4 and r = 3.

Using the formula for the sum:

S₆ = 4 × (1 - 3^6)/(1 - 3) = 4 × (1 - 729)/(-2) = 4 × (-728)/(-2) = 4 × 364 = 1,456

Therefore, the sum of the first 6 terms is 1,456.

Infinite Geometric Series

When dealing with geometric sequences, it's sometimes useful to consider the sum of all terms, extending infinitely. This is called an infinite geometric series.

For a geometric sequence with first term a₁ and common ratio r, the sum of the infinite geometric series is given by:

S∞ = a₁/(1 - r) (for |r| < 1)

This formula is only valid when the absolute value of r is less than 1 (|r| < 1), which ensures that the terms of the sequence approach zero as n increases, allowing the sum to converge to a finite value.

Example 9: Infinite Geometric Series

Find the sum of the infinite geometric series 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 + ...

First, we identify a₁ = 8 and r = 1/2 (since each term is half of the previous term).

Since |r| = 1/2 < 1, the infinite sum converges and we can use the formula:

S∞ = 8/(1 - 1/2) = 8/(1/2) = 8 × 2 = 16

Therefore, the sum of the infinite geometric series is 16.

Example 10: Repeating Decimals as Infinite Geometric Series

Express the repeating decimal 0.333... as a fraction in lowest terms.

We can write 0.333... as:

0.333... = 3/10 + 3/100 + 3/1000 + ...

This is an infinite geometric series with a₁ = 3/10 and r = 1/10.

Using the formula for the sum:

S∞ = (3/10)/(1 - 1/10) = (3/10)/(9/10) = (3/10) × (10/9) = 3/9 = 1/3

Therefore, 0.333... = 1/3.

Applications of Arithmetic and Geometric Sequences

Financial Applications

Arithmetic and geometric sequences have numerous applications in finance:

  • Simple Interest: The amount of interest earned forms an arithmetic sequence.
  • Compound Interest: The amount of money in an account with compound interest forms a geometric sequence.
  • Amortization: The remaining balance of a loan after each payment forms a sequence.
Example 11: Compound Interest

If you invest $1,000 at 5% interest compounded annually, how much will you have after 10 years?

The amount after n years forms a geometric sequence with a₁ = 1000 and r = 1.05 (representing 5% growth each year).

Using the formula for the nth term:

a₁₀ = 1000 × (1.05)^9 = 1000 × 1.55133 = $1,551.33

Therefore, after 10 years, the investment will be worth $1,551.33.

Physics Applications

Arithmetic and geometric sequences can also be applied in physics:

  • Uniformly Accelerated Motion: The distance traveled in consecutive equal time intervals forms an arithmetic sequence.
  • Damped Oscillations: The amplitude of consecutive oscillations in a damped system forms a geometric sequence.
  • Half-life: The amount of a radioactive substance after each half-life forms a geometric sequence.
Example 12: Radioactive Decay

A radioactive element has a half-life of 12 years. If we start with 100 grams of the element, how much will remain after 60 years?

The amount after each half-life forms a geometric sequence with a₁ = 100 and r = 0.5.

After 60 years, 60/12 = 5 half-lives have passed.

Using the formula for the nth term:

a₆ = 100 × (0.5)^5 = 100 × 0.03125 = 3.125 grams

Therefore, after 60 years, 3.125 grams of the radioactive element will remain.

Population Growth

Population growth can often be modeled using sequences:

  • Arithmetic Growth: When a population increases by a constant number each time period.
  • Geometric Growth: When a population increases by a constant percentage each time period (more common for biological populations).
Example 13: Population Growth

A city with a current population of 50,000 is growing at a rate of 3% per year. What will be the population after 15 years?

The population forms a geometric sequence with a₁ = 50,000 and r = 1.03 (representing 3% growth each year).

Using the formula for the nth term:

a₁₆ = 50,000 × (1.03)^15 = 50,000 × 1.557 = 77,850

Therefore, after 15 years, the city's population will be approximately 77,850.
