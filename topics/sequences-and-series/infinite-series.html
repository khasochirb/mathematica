Infinite Series

Exploring the sum of infinitely many terms and their convergence properties

Introduction to Infinite Series

An infinite series is the sum of infinitely many terms that follow a specific pattern. It is denoted by:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) aₙ = a₁ + a₂ + a₃ + ... + aₙ + ...

where {aₙ} is a sequence and the symbol Σ (sigma) represents summation. The infinite series is defined as the limit of the sequence of partial sums:

Sn = Σ(k=1 to n) aₖ = a₁ + a₂ + a₃ + ... + aₙ
S = Σ(n=1 to ∞) aₙ = lim(n→∞) Sn

If the limit of the sequence of partial sums exists and is finite, we say the series converges to that value. If the limit doesn't exist or is infinite, we say the series diverges.

Understanding the behavior of infinite series is crucial in calculus, physics, and many other fields of mathematics and science. In this lesson, we'll explore the concept of convergence and divergence of series and examine different types of infinite series and their properties.

Convergence and Divergence

Definitions and Basic Properties

For an infinite series Σ aₙ, we define the sequence of partial sums {Sn} as:

Sn = a₁ + a₂ + a₃ + ... + aₙ

Based on the behavior of this sequence, we classify series as follows:

  • Convergent Series: If the sequence of partial sums {Sn} converges to a finite limit S, then the series is said to converge to S. We write: Σ(n=1 to ∞) aₙ = S
  • Divergent Series: If the sequence of partial sums {Sn} does not converge to a finite limit, then the series is said to diverge.
Example 1: Geometric Series

Consider the geometric series Σ(n=0 to ∞) rn = 1 + r + r2 + r3 + ...

For |r| < 1, this series converges to 1/(1-r).

For |r| ≥ 1, this series diverges.

For instance, if r = 1/2, we have Σ(n=0 to ∞) (1/2)n = 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... = 2.

But if r = 2, we have Σ(n=0 to ∞) 2n = 1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + ..., which clearly grows without bound and thus diverges.

Necessary Condition for Convergence

If a series Σ aₙ converges, then the sequence {aₙ} must converge to 0. In other words:

If Σ(n=1 to ∞) aₙ converges, then lim(n→∞) aₙ = 0

This provides a useful test for divergence: if lim(n→∞) aₙ ≠ 0 or doesn't exist, then the series Σ aₙ diverges.

However, the converse is not true. If lim(n→∞) aₙ = 0, the series may still diverge. A classic example is the harmonic series:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/n = 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ...

Even though lim(n→∞) 1/n = 0, the harmonic series diverges.

Example 2: Testing for Divergence

Consider the series Σ(n=1 to ∞) n/(n+1).

We examine lim(n→∞) n/(n+1):

lim(n→∞) n/(n+1) = lim(n→∞) 1/(1+1/n) = 1

Since the limit of the terms is not 0, the series must diverge.

Special Series

p-Series

A p-series is a series of the form:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/np

where p is a constant. The convergence of a p-series depends on the value of p:

  • If p > 1, the series converges
  • If p ≤ 1, the series diverges
Example 3: p-Series Examples

The series Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/n2 is a p-series with p = 2 > 1, so it converges. In fact, it converges to π2/6 ≈ 1.645.

The series Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/n is a p-series with p = 1, so it diverges. This is the harmonic series mentioned earlier.

The series Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/√n is a p-series with p = 1/2 < 1, so it diverges.

Telescoping Series

A telescoping series has terms that can be expressed as the difference of two consecutive terms of a sequence, causing most terms to cancel out when the partial sums are computed.

If aₙ = bₙ - bₙ₊₁, then:

Σ(n=1 to N) aₙ = Σ(n=1 to N) (bₙ - bₙ₊₁) = b₁ - bN+1

For the infinite series to converge, we need lim(n→∞) bₙ to exist. In that case:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) (bₙ - bₙ₊₁) = b₁ - lim(n→∞) bₙ
Example 4: Telescoping Series

Consider the series Σ(n=1 to ∞) (1/n - 1/(n+1)).

We can rewrite each term: 1/n - 1/(n+1) = (n+1 - n)/(n(n+1)) = 1/(n(n+1))

But we also notice that 1/n - 1/(n+1) is exactly of the form bₙ - bₙ₊₁ with bₙ = 1/n.

So the partial sum SN = Σ(n=1 to N) (1/n - 1/(n+1)) = 1/1 - 1/(N+1) = 1 - 1/(N+1)

As N→∞, we get Σ(n=1 to ∞) (1/n - 1/(n+1)) = 1 - 0 = 1

Alternating Series

An alternating series has terms that alternate in sign. It can be expressed as:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) (-1)n+1an = a₁ - a₂ + a₃ - a₄ + ...

or

Σ(n=1 to ∞) (-1)nan = -a₁ + a₂ - a₃ + a₄ - ...

where an > 0 for all n.

The Alternating Series Test states that if {an} is a decreasing sequence with lim(n→∞) an = 0, then the alternating series Σ(n=1 to ∞) (-1)n+1an converges.

Example 5: Alternating Series

Consider the alternating harmonic series Σ(n=1 to ∞) (-1)n+1/n = 1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4 + ...

Here, an = 1/n is decreasing and lim(n→∞) 1/n = 0, so by the Alternating Series Test, this series converges.

In fact, this series converges to ln(2) ≈ 0.693.

Operations on Series

Algebraic Operations

If Σ aₙ and Σ bₙ are convergent series with sums A and B respectively, then:

  • Addition: Σ (aₙ + bₙ) = A + B
  • Subtraction: Σ (aₙ - bₙ) = A - B
  • Scalar Multiplication: Σ (c·aₙ) = c·A for any constant c
Example 6: Operations on Series

We know that Σ(n=0 to ∞) (1/2)n = 2 and Σ(n=0 to ∞) (1/3)n = 3/2.

Therefore:

Σ(n=0 to ∞) ((1/2)n + (1/3)n) = 2 + 3/2 = 7/2

Σ(n=0 to ∞) (2·(1/2)n) = 2·2 = 4

Reindexing

Changing the index of summation doesn't affect the convergence or value of the series, provided appropriate adjustments are made. For instance:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) aₙ = Σ(n=0 to ∞) an+1 = Σ(n=k to ∞) an-k+1
Example 7: Reindexing

Consider the series Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/2n = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ...

We can reindex this to start from n=0:

Σ(n=0 to ∞) 1/2n+1 = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ...

Or, we can factor out 1/2:

Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/2n = (1/2) · Σ(n=1 to ∞) 1/2n-1 = (1/2) · Σ(n=0 to ∞) 1/2n = (1/2) · 2 = 1

Multiplication of Series (Cauchy Product)

The product of two series is more complex and involves a special operation called the Cauchy product:

(Σ aₙ) · (Σ bₙ) = Σ cn

where cn = a₀bn + a₁bn-1 + ... + anb₀ = Σ(k=0 to n) akbn-k

If both series Σ aₙ and Σ bₙ are absolutely convergent, then their Cauchy product Σ cn converges to the product of their sums.

Example 8: Cauchy Product

Consider the product of two geometric series:

(1 + x + x2 + ...) · (1 + y + y2 + ...) = ?

Using the formula for the sum of a geometric series:

(1 + x + x2 + ...) = 1/(1-x) for |x| < 1

(1 + y + y2 + ...) = 1/(1-y) for |y| < 1

The product is (1/(1-x)) · (1/(1-y)) = 1/((1-x)(1-y))

Applications of Infinite Series

Taylor Series

One of the most important applications of infinite series is in representing functions as power series, specifically Taylor series:

f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)/1! + f''(a)(x-a)2/2! + f'''(a)(x-a)3/3! + ...

Or more concisely:

f(x) = Σ(n=0 to ∞) f(n)(a)(x-a)n/n!

When a = 0, we get the Maclaurin series:

f(x) = Σ(n=0 to ∞) f(n)(0)xn/n!
Example 9: Maclaurin Series

The Maclaurin series for ex is:

ex = 1 + x + x2/2! + x3/3! + ... = Σ(n=0 to ∞) xn/n!

The Maclaurin series for sin(x) is:

sin(x) = x - x3/3! + x5/5! - ... = Σ(n=0 to ∞) (-1)nx2n+1/(2n+1)!

Decimal Representations

Infinite series are used to represent decimal numbers. For instance, a decimal number like 0.123123123... can be represented as:

0.123123... = 123/103 · (1 + 1/103 + 1/106 + ...)

This is a geometric series with first term a = 123/103 and common ratio r = 1/103, so:

0.123123... = (123/103) / (1 - 1/103) = 123/999 = 41/333
Example 10: Decimal Representation

Express 0.999... as a fraction.

We can write 0.999... = 9/10 + 9/100 + 9/1000 + ...

This is a geometric series with a = 9/10 and r = 1/10.

Σ(n=1 to ∞) 9/10 · (1/10)n-1 = (9/10)/(1-1/10) = (9/10)/(9/10) = 1

Therefore, 0.999... = 1.

Physics and Engineering

Infinite series are frequently used in physics and engineering for approximating solutions to complex problems:

  • Fourier Series: Represents periodic functions as a sum of sines and cosines
  • Perturbation Theory: Approximates solutions to problems by adding small corrections
  • Circuit Analysis: Models the behavior of electrical circuits with reactive components
Example 11: Physics Application

In quantum mechanics, the energy levels of a harmonic oscillator are given by:

En = ħω(n + 1/2) for n = 0, 1, 2, ...

The partition function, which is used to calculate thermodynamic quantities, is:

Z = Σ(n=0 to ∞) e-En/kT = e-ħω/2kT · Σ(n=0 to ∞) (e-ħω/kT)n

This is a geometric series with r = e-ħω/kT, which converges for all temperatures T > 0:

Z = e-ħω/2kT / (1 - e-ħω/kT)
