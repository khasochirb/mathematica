Basic Probability Rules

Fundamental principles for measuring uncertainty and analyzing random phenomena

Introduction to Probability

Probability is the branch of mathematics concerned with numerical descriptions of how likely an event is to occur or how likely a proposition is to be true. The higher the probability of an event, the more likely it is to occur.

Historical Development

The mathematical theory of probability has its roots in the attempts to analyze games of chance by Gerolamo Cardano (16th century), Pierre de Fermat and Blaise Pascal (17th century). Today, probability theory has applications in numerous fields, including statistics, physics, engineering, biology, economics, and computer science.

Key Terminology

  • Experiment: Any procedure that can be repeated and has a well-defined set of possible outcomes.
  • Sample Space (Ω): The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  • Event: A subset of the sample space, representing a collection of outcomes.
  • Probability: A numerical measure of the likelihood of an event, ranging from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).
Example: Coin Toss Experiment

For a single coin toss:

The sample space is Ω = {Heads, Tails}.

The event "getting heads" is E = {Heads}.

If the coin is fair, the probability of getting heads is P(E) = 0.5 or 1/2.

Probability Axioms and Basic Properties

Modern probability theory is based on a set of axioms proposed by Andrey Kolmogorov in 1933. These axioms serve as the foundation for all probability calculations.

Kolmogorov's Axioms

For a probability measure P on a sample space Ω:

  1. Non-negativity: For any event A, P(A) ≥ 0.
  2. Normalization: The probability of the entire sample space is 1, i.e., P(Ω) = 1.
  3. Countable Additivity: For a countable collection of pairwise disjoint events A₁, A₂, A₃, ..., the probability of their union equals the sum of their individual probabilities: P(A₁ ∪ A₂ ∪ A₃ ∪ ...) = P(A₁) + P(A₂) + P(A₃) + ...

Basic Properties

From these axioms, several useful properties can be derived:

P(∅) = 0 (The probability of an impossible event is 0)
P(A') = 1 - P(A) (The probability of the complement of A)
If A ⊆ B, then P(A) ≤ P(B) (Monotonicity)
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B) (Addition rule)
Example: Applying Basic Properties

In a deck of 52 cards, find the probability of drawing either a heart or a face card (J, Q, K).

Let A = event of drawing a heart, and B = event of drawing a face card.

P(A) = 13/52 = 1/4 (There are 13 hearts in a deck)

P(B) = 12/52 = 3/13 (There are 12 face cards in a deck: 3 in each suit)

P(A ∩ B) = 3/52 (There are 3 face cards that are also hearts)

Using the addition rule: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B) = 1/4 + 3/13 - 3/52 = 13/52 + 12/52 - 3/52 = 22/52 = 11/26

Probability Calculation Methods

Classical Probability (Equally Likely Outcomes)

When all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely, the probability of an event A is calculated as:

P(A) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes
Example: Classical Probability

In a traditional Mongolian shagai game, a player throws four ankle bones (shagai). Each bone can land in one of four positions: horse (mor'), sheep (hon'), goat (iama), or camel (temee). If we assume each position is equally likely, what is the probability of getting a sheep?

Since there are 4 possible outcomes for each bone and we're interested in the sheep position:

P(sheep) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes = 1/4 = 0.25

So there's a 25% chance of getting a sheep on a single throw.

Relative Frequency Approach

In this approach, probability is estimated based on observed frequency in repeated trials:

P(A) ≈ Number of times A occurred / Total number of trials

As the number of trials increases, the relative frequency tends to converge to the true probability (Law of Large Numbers).

Example: Relative Frequency

A group of Mongolian children played a shagai game 100 times, recording the number of times they got a horse. They observed 28 horses in these 100 throws. Based on this data, we can estimate the probability of getting a horse as:

P(horse) = Number of observed horses / Total number of throws = 28/100 = 0.28

This empirical probability of 0.28 suggests that horses might be slightly more common than other outcomes in this particular game.

Subjective Probability

Subjective probability represents a personal judgment about the likelihood of an event, often used when classical or frequency approaches are not applicable.

Example: Subjective Probability

A meteorologist might assign a 70% probability of rain tomorrow based on current atmospheric conditions, historical data, and professional experience.

Probability Rules and Formulas

Complement Rule

The probability of an event not occurring is one minus the probability of the event occurring:

P(A') = 1 - P(A)
Example: Complement Rule

In a traditional Mongolian archery competition, an archer has a 0.7 probability of hitting the target. What is the probability of missing the target?

Using the complement rule:

P(miss) = 1 - P(hit) = 1 - 0.7 = 0.3

So there's a 30% chance of missing the target.

Addition Rule (Union of Events)

For any two events A and B:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

For mutually exclusive events (A ∩ B = ∅), this simplifies to:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
Example: Addition Rule

In a Mongolian wrestling tournament, there are 16 wrestlers. The probability of a wrestler winning their first match is 0.5, and the probability of winning their second match is 0.4. What is the probability of a wrestler winning either their first or second match?

Using the addition rule:

P(first or second win) = P(first win) + P(second win) - P(both wins)

P(first or second win) = 0.5 + 0.4 - (0.5 × 0.4) = 0.9 - 0.2 = 0.7

So there's a 70% chance of winning either the first or second match.

Multiplication Rule (Intersection of Events)

For any two events A and B:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A|B) × P(B) = P(B|A) × P(A)

where P(A|B) is the conditional probability of A given B.

For independent events, this simplifies to:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
Example: Multiplication Rule

In a traditional Mongolian card game, a player draws two cards from a deck. What is the probability of drawing two aces if the first card is not replaced?

Using the multiplication rule:

P(two aces) = P(first ace) × P(second ace | first ace)

P(two aces) = (4/52) × (3/51) = 12/2652 ≈ 0.0045

So there's about a 0.45% chance of drawing two aces in succession.

Independence and Dependence

Independent Events

Two events A and B are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. Mathematically, events A and B are independent if and only if:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

Equivalently, A and B are independent if:

P(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B)
Example: Independent Events

Consider tossing a coin twice. Let A be the event "first toss is heads" and B be the event "second toss is heads".

P(A) = 1/2, P(B) = 1/2, and P(A ∩ B) = 1/4

Since P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4, events A and B are independent.

Dependent Events

Events are dependent if the occurrence of one affects the probability of the other. For dependent events:

P(A ∩ B) ≠ P(A) × P(B)
Example: Dependent Events

Drawing cards from a deck without replacement: Let A be "first card is a heart" and B be "second card is a heart".

P(A) = 13/52 = 1/4

P(B|A) = 12/51 (After drawing a heart, 12 hearts remain out of 51 cards)

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A) = 1/4 × 12/51 = 12/204 = 1/17

Since P(A ∩ B) ≠ P(A) × P(B) (note that P(B) would be 1/4), events A and B are dependent.

Counting Methods in Probability

Basic Counting Principle

If there are m ways to perform task 1 and n ways to perform task 2, then there are m × n ways to perform both tasks in sequence.

Example: Basic Counting Principle

A restaurant offers 5 appetizers, 8 main courses, and 4 desserts. How many different three-course meals are possible?

Number of possible meals = 5 × 8 × 4 = 160

Permutations

A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. The number of permutations of n distinct objects is:

P(n) = n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 2 × 1

The number of permutations of r objects selected from n distinct objects is:

P(n,r) = n! / (n-r)! = n × (n-1) × ... × (n-r+1)
Example: Permutations

In how many ways can 3 people be selected from a group of 10 people to fill the positions of president, vice president, and secretary?

P(10,3) = 10! / (10-3)! = 10! / 7! = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720

Combinations

A combination is a selection of objects without regard to order. The number of ways to select r objects from n distinct objects is:

C(n,r) = n! / (r! × (n-r)!) = P(n,r) / r!
Example: Combinations

In how many ways can a committee of 3 people be formed from a group of 10 people?

C(10,3) = 10! / (3! × 7!) = 10 × 9 × 8 / (3 × 2 × 1) = 720 / 6 = 120
