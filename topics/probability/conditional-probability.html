Conditional Probability

Understanding how the probability of an event changes when we have additional information

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Probability » Conditional Probability

Introduction to Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It represents how knowledge of one event affects the likelihood of another event.

Definition and Notation

The conditional probability of event A given that event B has occurred is denoted as P(A|B) and is defined as:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), where P(B) > 0

This can be intuitively understood as restricting the sample space to only those outcomes where B occurs, and then finding the probability of A within this restricted sample space.

Example: Rolling a Die

Consider rolling a fair six-sided die. What is the probability of rolling an even number given that the number rolled is greater than 3?

Let A = {rolling an even number} = {2, 4, 6}

Let B = {rolling a number greater than 3} = {4, 5, 6}

P(B) = 3/6 = 1/2

A ∩ B = {4, 6} (numbers that are both even and greater than 3)

P(A ∩ B) = 2/6 = 1/3

Therefore, P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) = (1/3) / (1/2) = 2/3

Why Conditional Probability Matters

Conditional probability is crucial because most real-world scenarios involve making decisions based on partial information. Understanding how new information changes probabilities is essential in fields ranging from medicine (diagnostic testing) to finance (risk assessment) to artificial intelligence (Bayesian networks).

Multiplication Rule and Tree Diagrams

Multiplication Rule

The multiplication rule follows directly from the definition of conditional probability and provides a way to find the probability of the intersection of events:

P(A ∩ B) = P(B) × P(A|B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

For multiple events, this extends to:

P(A ∩ B ∩ C) = P(A) × P(B|A) × P(C|A ∩ B)
Example: Drawing Cards

What is the probability of drawing two aces in succession from a standard deck of 52 cards, without replacement?

Let A₁ = {first card is an ace} and A₂ = {second card is an ace}

P(A₁) = 4/52 = 1/13 (there are 4 aces in a deck of 52 cards)

P(A₂|A₁) = 3/51 (after drawing one ace, 3 aces remain out of 51 cards)

P(A₁ ∩ A₂) = P(A₁) × P(A₂|A₁) = (1/13) × (3/51) = 3/663 = 1/221

Tree Diagrams

Tree diagrams provide a visual representation of sequential events and are particularly useful for calculating conditional probabilities. Each branch in the tree represents a possible outcome, and the probabilities along each path are multiplied to find the probability of that specific sequence of events.

Example: Tree Diagram for Two Coin Tosses

Consider tossing a fair coin twice. We can represent this with a tree diagram:

First toss: H (0.5) or T (0.5)

From H: Second toss H (0.5) or T (0.5)

From T: Second toss H (0.5) or T (0.5)

The four possible outcomes are:

H-H: P = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

H-T: P = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

T-H: P = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

T-T: P = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

Tree diagrams are especially useful for solving complex problems involving multiple stages or when the probability at each stage depends on the outcomes of previous stages.

Law of Total Probability

The Law of Total Probability allows us to calculate the total probability of an event by considering all the ways it can occur through different mutually exclusive scenarios.

Statement of the Law

If {B₁, B₂, ..., Bₙ} is a partition of the sample space (i.e., the events are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive), then for any event A:

P(A) = P(A|B₁)P(B₁) + P(A|B₂)P(B₂) + ... + P(A|Bₙ)P(Bₙ)

In more compact notation:

P(A) = Σᵢ P(A|Bᵢ)P(Bᵢ)
Example: Medical Testing

A medical test for a disease has a 95% probability of a positive result when tested on a person with the disease (true positive) and a 10% probability of a positive result when tested on a person without the disease (false positive). Assume that 2% of the population has the disease. What is the probability of a randomly selected person testing positive?

Let D = {person has the disease}, D' = {person does not have the disease}, and T = {test is positive}

Given:

P(T|D) = 0.95 (sensitivity)

P(T|D') = 0.10 (false positive rate)

P(D) = 0.02 (prevalence)

P(D') = 0.98

Using the Law of Total Probability:

P(T) = P(T|D)P(D) + P(T|D')P(D')

P(T) = 0.95 × 0.02 + 0.10 × 0.98

P(T) = 0.019 + 0.098 = 0.117

Therefore, the probability of a randomly selected person testing positive is 0.117 or 11.7%.

Importance in Complex Problems

The Law of Total Probability is particularly valuable when dealing with complex scenarios where the direct computation of probabilities is difficult, but the conditional probabilities are known or easier to determine. It forms the basis for many probabilistic methods, including Bayesian statistics and decision theory.

Independence of Events

Definition of Independence

Two events A and B are independent if the occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other. Mathematically, A and B are independent if:

P(A|B) = P(A) or equivalently, P(B|A) = P(B)

Using the definition of conditional probability, this can also be expressed as:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
Example: Independence of Events

Consider rolling two fair dice. Let A = {sum is 7} and B = {first die shows 3}. Are these events independent?

P(A) = 6/36 = 1/6 (there are 6 ways to get a sum of 7 out of 36 possible outcomes)

P(B) = 6/36 = 1/6 (the first die shows 3 in 6 of the 36 possible outcomes)

A ∩ B = {(3,4)} (the only outcome where the first die shows 3 and the sum is 7)

P(A ∩ B) = 1/36

If the events were independent, we would have P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) = (1/6) × (1/6) = 1/36

Since P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B), events A and B are indeed independent.

Conditional Independence

Two events A and B are conditionally independent given a third event C if:

P(A ∩ B|C) = P(A|C) × P(B|C)

It's important to note that conditional independence given C does not imply unconditional independence, and vice versa.

Example: Conditional Independence

Consider a study examining the relationship between smoking (S), lung cancer (L), and having a genetic marker (G). The events L and G might be dependent in the general population, but given that someone is a smoker (conditioning on S), L and G might be conditionally independent.

If P(L ∩ G|S) = P(L|S) × P(G|S), then L and G are conditionally independent given S.

Multiple Independent Events

For more than two events, independence gets more complex. A collection of events is mutually independent if every event is independent of any combination of the other events. For three events A, B, and C to be mutually independent, we need:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
P(A ∩ C) = P(A) × P(C)
P(B ∩ C) = P(B) × P(C)
P(A ∩ B ∩ C) = P(A) × P(B) × P(C)

Applications of Conditional Probability

Medical Diagnosis

Conditional probability plays a crucial role in medical diagnosis, where test results need to be interpreted in the context of disease prevalence.

Key terms in medical testing:

  • Sensitivity: P(positive test | disease) - probability of a positive test given the person has the disease
  • Specificity: P(negative test | no disease) - probability of a negative test given the person does not have the disease
  • Positive Predictive Value (PPV): P(disease | positive test) - probability of having the disease given a positive test
  • Negative Predictive Value (NPV): P(no disease | negative test) - probability of not having the disease given a negative test
Example: Medical Diagnosis

A diagnostic test for a rare disease has a sensitivity of 99% and a specificity of 95%. The disease affects 1 in 1000 people. If a person tests positive, what is the probability they actually have the disease?

Let D = {person has the disease} and T = {test is positive}

Given:

P(T|D) = 0.99 (sensitivity)

P(T'|D') = 0.95 (specificity), so P(T|D') = 0.05

P(D) = 0.001 (prevalence)

We want to find P(D|T) (the positive predictive value). Using Bayes' theorem:

P(D|T) = [P(T|D) × P(D)] / [P(T|D) × P(D) + P(T|D') × P(D')]

P(D|T) = [0.99 × 0.001] / [0.99 × 0.001 + 0.05 × 0.999]

P(D|T) = 0.00099 / (0.00099 + 0.04995) = 0.00099 / 0.05094 ≈ 0.0194

Therefore, despite the high sensitivity and specificity, the probability that a person who tests positive actually has the disease is only about 1.94%. This illustrates the "base rate fallacy" where the rarity of the condition significantly affects the predictive value of the test.

Risk Assessment and Decision Making

Conditional probability is fundamental to risk assessment and decision making under uncertainty. It helps us update our beliefs as new information becomes available, allowing for more informed decisions.

Example: Insurance Risk Assessment

An insurance company has data showing that among all drivers, 1% will have an accident in a given year. Among drivers with a speeding ticket, 5% will have an accident in a given year. If 10% of all drivers get speeding tickets, what percentage of accidents involve drivers with speeding tickets?

Let A = {driver has an accident} and S = {driver has a speeding ticket}

Given:

P(A) = 0.01

P(A|S) = 0.05

P(S) = 0.10

We want to find P(S|A). Using Bayes' theorem:

P(S|A) = [P(A|S) × P(S)] / P(A)

P(S|A) = [0.05 × 0.10] / 0.01 = 0.005 / 0.01 = 0.5

Therefore, 50% of all accidents involve drivers with speeding tickets, although only 10% of drivers have speeding tickets.
