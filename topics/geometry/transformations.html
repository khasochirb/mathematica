Transformations in Geometry

Understanding rotations, reflections, translations, and their applications

Home » Learning Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Geometry » Transformations

Introduction to Geometric Transformations

Geometric transformations are operations that change the position, size, or orientation of a geometric figure while preserving certain properties. These transformations are fundamental to understanding symmetry, congruence, and similarity, and they have applications in computer graphics, physics, engineering, and various branches of mathematics.

Types of Transformations

Transformations can be classified into different categories based on the properties they preserve:

  • Isometries (or Rigid Transformations): Preserve distance and angle measures, resulting in congruent figures (e.g., translations, rotations, reflections)
  • Similarity Transformations: Preserve angle measures but not necessarily distances, resulting in similar figures (e.g., dilations combined with isometries)
  • Affine Transformations: Preserve parallelism but not necessarily angles or distances
  • Projective Transformations: Preserve collinearity (points on a line remain on a line) but not necessarily parallelism

In this page, we will focus primarily on isometries (rigid transformations) and dilations, which are the most commonly studied in basic geometry.

Translations

A translation is a transformation that moves every point of a figure the same distance in the same direction. It can be thought of as "sliding" the figure without rotating or reflecting it.

Properties of Translations

  • Preserves distance (the distance between any two points remains the same)
  • Preserves angle measures
  • Preserves orientation (clockwise arrangements remain clockwise)
  • Results in congruent figures

Mathematical Representation

In coordinate geometry, a translation by a vector (h, k) transforms a point (x, y) to (x + h, y + k).

Translation by vector (h, k):

(x, y) → (x + h, y + k)

In matrix form, this can be written as:

[x'] = [x] + [h]

[y'] [y] [k]

Example: Translation of a Traditional Ger Pattern

Consider a traditional Mongolian ger door pattern with vertices A(1, 2), B(3, 2), and C(2, 4).

To translate this pattern by the vector (3, −1) to create a repeating design on the ger's wall, we add 3 to each x-coordinate and subtract 1 from each y-coordinate:

A(1, 2) → A'(1 + 3, 2 − 1) = A'(4, 1)

B(3, 2) → B'(3 + 3, 2 − 1) = B'(6, 1)

C(2, 4) → C'(2 + 3, 4 − 1) = C'(5, 3)

The translated pattern A'B'C' maintains the same shape, size, and orientation as the original pattern ABC, creating a beautiful repeating design typical in Mongolian ger decorations.

Rotations

A rotation is a transformation that turns a figure around a fixed point, called the center of rotation, through a specified angle.

Properties of Rotations

  • Preserves distance (the distance between any two points remains the same)
  • Preserves angle measures
  • Preserves orientation (clockwise arrangements remain clockwise)
  • Results in congruent figures
  • The center of rotation remains fixed (does not move)

Mathematical Representation

For a rotation around the origin by an angle θ (counterclockwise), the coordinates transform as follows:

Rotation by angle θ around the origin:

(x, y) → (x cos θ − y sin θ, x sin θ + y cos θ)

In matrix form:

[x'] = [cos θ −sin θ] [x]

[y'] [sin θ cos θ] [y]

For a rotation around a point (a, b) other than the origin:

  1. Translate the center of rotation to the origin: (x, y) → (x − a, y − b)
  2. Rotate around the origin
  3. Translate back: (x', y') → (x' + a, y' + b)
Example: Rotation of a Traditional Mongolian Symbol

Rotate the point P(3, 2) of a traditional Mongolian soyombo symbol by 90° counterclockwise around the origin.

Using the rotation formula with θ = 90°:

x' = x cos 90° − y sin 90° = 3 × 0 − 2 × 1 = −2

y' = x sin 90° + y cos 90° = 3 × 1 + 2 × 0 = 3

Therefore, P(3, 2) rotates to P'(−2, 3).

We can verify that the distance from the origin is preserved: √(3² + 2²) = √(13) = √((−2)² + 3²).

This rotation is similar to how the soyombo symbol appears on the Mongolian flag, maintaining its proportions while rotating.

Reflections

A reflection is a transformation that flips a figure across a line, called the line of reflection or mirror line. It creates a mirror image of the original figure.

Properties of Reflections

  • Preserves distance (the distance between any two points remains the same)
  • Preserves angle measures
  • Reverses orientation (clockwise arrangements become counterclockwise)
  • Results in congruent figures
  • Points on the line of reflection remain fixed
  • The line of reflection is the perpendicular bisector of the line segment joining a point and its reflection

Mathematical Representation

For reflection across the x-axis:

(x, y) → (x, −y)

For reflection across the y-axis:

(x, y) → (−x, y)

For reflection across the line y = x:

(x, y) → (y, x)

For reflection across a general line y = mx + b, the transformation is more complex and involves projecting the point onto the line and then extending an equal distance on the other side.

Example: Reflection of a Traditional Mongolian Ornament

Reflect a traditional Mongolian knot pattern ABC with vertices A(1, 3), B(4, 5), and C(2, 6) across the x-axis to create a symmetrical design.

For reflection across the x-axis, we negate the y-coordinates:

A(1, 3) → A'(1, −3)

B(4, 5) → B'(4, −5)

C(2, 6) → C'(2, −6)

The reflected pattern A'B'C' has the same shape and size as ABC, but its orientation is reversed, creating a balanced symmetrical design commonly found in Mongolian traditional art.

Dilations (Scaling)

A dilation is a transformation that changes the size of a figure without changing its shape. It either enlarges or reduces the figure by a scale factor from a fixed point called the center of dilation.

Properties of Dilations

  • Preserves angle measures
  • Preserves orientation
  • Multiplies all distances by the scale factor
  • Results in similar but not necessarily congruent figures

Mathematical Representation

For a dilation from the origin with scale factor k:

(x, y) → (kx, ky)

For a dilation from a point (a, b) with scale factor k:

  1. Translate the center of dilation to the origin: (x, y) → (x − a, y − b)
  2. Apply the dilation: (x − a, y − b) → (k(x − a), k(y − b))
  3. Translate back: (k(x − a), k(y − b)) → (k(x − a) + a, k(y − b) + b)
  4. Simplify: (x, y) → (a + k(x − a), b + k(y − b))
Example: Dilation of a Ger Door Frame

Dilate a traditional Mongolian ger door frame PQRS with vertices P(1, 1), Q(5, 1), R(5, 3), and S(1, 3) by a factor of 2 from the origin to create a larger entrance.

For dilation from the origin with scale factor k = 2, we multiply each coordinate by 2:

P(1, 1) → P'(2 × 1, 2 × 1) = P'(2, 2)

Q(5, 1) → Q'(2 × 5, 2 × 1) = Q'(10, 2)

R(5, 3) → R'(2 × 5, 2 × 3) = R'(10, 6)

S(1, 3) → S'(2 × 1, 2 × 3) = S'(2, 6)

The dilated door frame P'Q'R'S' has the same proportions as PQRS but is twice as large in all dimensions, maintaining the traditional ger's architectural style while accommodating larger entrances.

Composite Transformations

A composite transformation is a sequence of two or more transformations applied one after another. The order in which transformations are applied matters, as the result may differ depending on the sequence.

Properties of Composite Transformations

  • The composition of two or more isometries is an isometry
  • The composition of two or more similarity transformations is a similarity transformation
  • Non-commutativity: In general, A followed by B doesn't give the same result as B followed by A

Common Composite Transformations

Some important composite transformations include:

  • Glide Reflection: A reflection followed by a translation parallel to the line of reflection
  • Half-turn: A rotation by 180°, equivalent to a composition of two reflections across perpendicular lines
  • Rotation: Can be viewed as a composition of two reflections across intersecting lines (the angle of rotation is twice the angle between the lines)
Example: Composite Transformation of a Traditional Pattern

Apply a rotation of 90° counterclockwise about the origin followed by a translation of (2, 3) to a point P(1, 2) of a traditional Mongolian geometric pattern.

First, rotate P(1, 2) by 90° counterclockwise about the origin:

x' = 1 cos 90° − 2 sin 90° = 0 − 2 = −2

y' = 1 sin 90° + 2 cos 90° = 1 + 0 = 1

So P rotates to P'(−2, 1).

Next, translate P'(−2, 1) by (2, 3):

x'' = −2 + 2 = 0

y'' = 1 + 3 = 4

Therefore, the final position is P''(0, 4).

This transformation sequence is similar to how traditional Mongolian artisans create complex repeating patterns in their decorative arts.

Applications of Transformations

Symmetry

Transformations are essential for describing and understanding symmetry in geometric figures:

  • Reflection Symmetry (Line Symmetry): A figure has reflection symmetry if it remains unchanged after reflection across a line
  • Rotational Symmetry: A figure has rotational symmetry if it remains unchanged after rotation by a certain angle around a point
  • Translation Symmetry: Common in patterns and tilings, where the pattern remains unchanged after translation

Computer Graphics

Transformations are fundamental in computer graphics for manipulating objects on screen:

  • Moving, rotating, and scaling objects
  • Animation effects and transitions
  • 3D rendering and projection
  • Creating visual effects in games and simulations

Architecture and Art

Transformations have been used in architecture and art throughout history:

  • Creating repeating patterns in Islamic art and architecture
  • Tessellations in floor and wall designs
  • Perspective transformations in Renaissance art
  • Symmetrical designs in building facades

Physics and Engineering

Transformations have important applications in physics and engineering:

  • Coordinate transformations between reference frames
  • Transformation of electrical circuit elements
  • Mechanical linkages and mechanisms
  • Robotics and kinematics
Example: Symmetry in Traditional Mongolian Patterns

A traditional Mongolian geometric pattern has multiple types of symmetry:

It has 6 lines of reflection symmetry (through each vertex and the midpoint of the opposite side), similar to the patterns found in traditional Mongolian textiles.

It has rotational symmetry of order 6, meaning it looks the same after rotation by multiples of 60°, like the repeating patterns in Mongolian traditional art.

These symmetry properties can be described using the transformations we've discussed.

The symmetry group of the pattern, denoted D₆, consists of 12 symmetry operations: 6 rotations and 6 reflections, creating the intricate designs found in Mongolian cultural artifacts.
