Areas & Volumes

Calculating the measures of two-dimensional and three-dimensional shapes

Home » Resources » Mathematics Roadmap » Geometry » Areas & Volumes

Introduction to Measurement

Calculating areas and volumes is a fundamental aspect of geometry with extensive applications in science, engineering, architecture, and everyday life. These measurements allow us to quantify space in both two and three dimensions.

Key Concepts

  • Area: The measure of the space enclosed by a two-dimensional shape, measured in square units (e.g., cm², m², km²)
  • Volume: The measure of the space enclosed by a three-dimensional shape, measured in cubic units (e.g., cm³, m³, km³)
  • Surface Area: The total area of all external surfaces of a three-dimensional object

In this page, we will explore formulas and techniques for calculating these measurements for various geometric shapes and solids, along with applications and problem-solving strategies.

Areas of Basic Plane Figures

Polygons

Rectangle:

Area = length × width

A = l × w

Square:

Area = side² = s²

Triangle:

Area = (1/2) × base × height

A = (1/2) × b × h

Using Heron's formula (when three sides are known):

Area = √(s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c))

where s = (a + b + c)/2 (the semi-perimeter)

Parallelogram:

Area = base × height

A = b × h

Rhombus:

Area = (1/2) × product of diagonals

A = (1/2) × d₁ × d₂

Trapezoid:

Area = (1/2) × (sum of parallel sides) × height

A = (1/2) × (a + c) × h

Regular Polygon (n sides):

Area = (1/2) × perimeter × apothem

A = (1/2) × n × s × a

where s is the side length and a is the apothem (distance from center to midpoint of any side)

Area = (n × s²)/(4 × tan(π/n))

Curved Figures

Circle:

Area = π × radius²

A = πr²

Semicircle:

Area = (π × r²)/2

Circular Sector (angle θ in radians):

Area = (1/2) × r² × θ

Circular Segment:

Area = (r²/2) × (θ - sin θ)

where θ is the central angle in radians

Ellipse:

Area = π × a × b

where a and b are the semi-major and semi-minor axes

Example: Finding Areas of Complex Shapes

Find the area of a traditional Mongolian ger door design consisting of a square with side length 6 cm and a semicircle attached to one side of the square, representing the traditional arched doorway.

Solution:

Area of square = s² = 6² = 36 cm²

Area of semicircle = (πr²)/2 = (π × 3²)/2 = (9π)/2 ≈ 14.14 cm²

Total area = 36 + 14.14 = 50.14 cm²

This represents the area needed to create the traditional arched doorway pattern found in Mongolian gers.

Advanced Area Calculations

Area by Decomposition

Complex shapes can be decomposed into simpler shapes whose areas can be calculated and then added.

Area by Coordinates (Shoelace Formula)

For a polygon with vertices (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), ..., (xₙ, yₙ) listed in order around the perimeter:

Area = (1/2) |∑ᵢ₌₁ⁿ (xᵢyᵢ₊₁ - xᵢ₊₁yᵢ)|

where (xₙ₊₁, yₙ₊₁) = (x₁, y₁)

Area by Integration

For a region bounded by curves y = f(x), y = g(x), x = a, and x = b, where f(x) ≥ g(x) for all x in [a, b]:

Area = ∫ₐᵇ [f(x) - g(x)] dx

Area by Vector Product

The area of a parallelogram with sides represented by vectors u and v is:

Area = |u × v|

The area of a triangle with vertices A, B, and C is:

Area = (1/2) |AB × AC|

Example: Area Using Shoelace Formula

Find the area of a traditional Mongolian decorative pattern in the shape of a quadrilateral with vertices at (1, 1), (3, 2), (4, 5), and (2, 3).

Solution using the Shoelace formula:

A = (1/2) |(1×2 + 3×5 + 4×3 + 2×1) - (1×3 + 3×4 + 4×2 + 2×1)|

A = (1/2) |(2 + 15 + 12 + 2) - (3 + 12 + 8 + 2)|

A = (1/2) |31 - 25| = (1/2) × 6 = 3 square units

This area represents a traditional geometric pattern commonly found in Mongolian textile designs.

Volumes of 3D Solids

Polyhedra

Rectangular Prism (Cuboid):

Volume = length × width × height

V = l × w × h

Cube:

Volume = side³ = s³

Triangular Prism:

Volume = area of triangular base × height

V = (1/2) × b × h × L

where L is the length of the prism

General Right Prism:

Volume = area of base × height

V = B × h

Pyramid:

Volume = (1/3) × area of base × height

V = (1/3) × B × h

Tetrahedron (Regular):

Volume = (√2/12) × s³

where s is the side length

Curved Solids

Cylinder:

Volume = area of circular base × height

V = πr² × h

Cone:

Volume = (1/3) × area of circular base × height

V = (1/3) × πr² × h

Sphere:

Volume = (4/3) × π × radius³

V = (4/3) × πr³

Hemisphere:

Volume = (2/3) × π × radius³

V = (2/3) × πr³

Ellipsoid:

Volume = (4/3) × π × a × b × c

where a, b, and c are the semi-principal axes

Example: Volume of a Composite Solid

Find the volume of a traditional Mongolian airag container consisting of a cylinder with radius 4 cm and height 10 cm, topped with a hemisphere of radius 4 cm, representing the traditional curved lid.

Solution:

Volume of cylinder = πr² × h = π × 4² × 10 = 160π cm³

Volume of hemisphere = (2/3) × πr³ = (2/3) × π × 4³ = (2/3) × 64π = 42.67π cm³

Total volume = 160π + 42.67π = 202.67π ≈ 636.8 cm³

This represents the capacity of a traditional Mongolian airag container, which typically holds about 0.64 liters of the fermented beverage.

Surface Areas

Polyhedra

Rectangular Prism:

Surface Area = 2(lw + lh + wh)

where l, w, and h are the length, width, and height

Cube:

Surface Area = 6s²

where s is the side length

General Right Prism:

Surface Area = 2B + Ph

where B is the area of the base, P is the perimeter of the base, and h is the height

Pyramid:

Surface Area = B + (1/2) × P × L

where B is the area of the base, P is the perimeter of the base, and L is the slant height

Curved Solids

Cylinder:

Surface Area = 2πr² + 2πrh

= 2πr(r + h)

Cone:

Surface Area = πr² + πrl

= πr(r + l)

where l is the slant height: l = √(r² + h²)

Sphere:

Surface Area = 4πr²

Hemisphere:

Surface Area = 3πr²

(including the circular base)

Example: Surface Area of a Triangular Prism

Find the surface area of a traditional Mongolian decorative panel in the shape of a triangular prism with an equilateral triangular base of side 6 cm and a height of 10 cm.

Solution:

Area of each triangular face = (√3/4) × s² = (√3/4) × 6² = 9√3 cm²

Area of the three rectangular faces = 3 × 6 × 10 = 180 cm²

Total surface area = 2 × 9√3 + 180 = 18√3 + 180 ≈ 211.2 cm²

This represents the area needed to create a traditional Mongolian decorative panel, which could be used in ger construction or as a standalone decorative element.

Applications and Advanced Techniques

Cavalieri's Principle

If two solids have equal cross-sectional areas at every height, then they have the same volume. This principle is useful for deriving volume formulas and solving complex problems.

The Method of Slicing

For solids with varying cross-sections, the volume can be calculated using integration:

V = ∫ₐᵇ A(x) dx

where A(x) is the area of the cross-section at position x

The Method of Shells

For solids of revolution around the y-axis:

V = 2π ∫ₐᵇ x f(x) dx

Practical Applications

  • Architecture and Construction: Calculating building materials, paint coverage, floor space
  • Manufacturing: Determining material requirements for products
  • Fluid Dynamics: Calculating container capacities, flow rates
  • Geology: Estimating mineral deposits, water reservoirs
  • Packaging Design: Optimizing packaging to minimize material while protecting contents
Example: Optimization Problem

Find the dimensions of a traditional Mongolian airag container (cylindrical vessel) with volume 500 cm³ that uses the minimum amount of material (surface area), while maintaining traditional proportions.

Solution:

Let r be the radius and h be the height of the cylinder.

The volume constraint gives us: πr²h = 500

So h = 500/(πr²)

The surface area is: S = 2πr² + 2πrh = 2πr² + 2πr(500/(πr²)) = 2πr² + 1000/r

To minimize S, we find where dS/dr = 0:

dS/dr = 4πr - 1000/r² = 0

So 4πr³ = 1000, which gives r ≈ 4.3 cm

And h = 500/(π×4.3²) ≈ 8.6 cm

The optimal dimensions are r ≈ 4.3 cm and h ≈ 8.6 cm, which gives a height equal to twice the radius, matching traditional Mongolian container proportions.
